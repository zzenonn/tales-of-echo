Auri

The library had always been Auri’s refuge. Tonight, she sat at one of the long oak tables near the back, books spread out in a careful semicircle around her like wards. Some were open, others stacked and tabbed, a few already containing notes stuffed in the pages written in her precise, compact script. At a glance, it looked orderly, intentional, and controlled.

It wasn’t.

Her attention moved restlessly from page to page: a volume on individual founders of Spark Oath to fulfill the obligations bestowed upon her by Artorius; another about arcane rubies that might help Caspian contact her mentor; glamour spells for Sol and Bookie; and a novel she’d checked out specifically for her next meeting with Vornakir. She’d read the same paragraph twice without absorbing it, then caught herself and exhaled softly through her nose.

Artorius’ task lingered on the edge of her thoughts: the book, Termina Edict, hidden in the cellar, the one he’d trusted her to find. There was the upcoming spell duel. Homework for classes. Part-time jobs and club meetings. And layered over all of it was the Chroma Clash, which demanded strategy and foresight.

Preparation, a good cup of tea, and her favorite corner of the library usually soothed her, but tonight, they only made the weight more noticeable. Auri caught herself biting her bottom lip and stilled, forcing her jaw to relax. Her tail, which had been curling and uncurling beneath the table, gave an impatient swish before she looped it around the leg of her chair to keep it still.

Anyone passing by would see a composed scholar deep in study. Focused, capable, and unbothered. Anyone who knew her well would notice the tells: the way she worried her lip, the way she swished her tail, the untouched cup of tea cooling beside her for nearly an hour.

The right people don’t see you as too much.

Redd’s practiced smile didn’t even get the chance to form this time.

It just collapsed.

His breath caught sharply in his chest, like he’d misjudged a step. He blinked once, twice; fast, irritated, as if he could blink the sting away.

He couldn’t.

When she pulled him into the embrace, he didn’t resist.

For half a second, he stayed stiff—like he didn’t know what to do with it. And then something in him gave way completely.

The first tear slipped before he could stop it.

Then another.

And then it wasn’t contained anymore.

Redd didn’t cry quietly.

It came in uneven, broken breaths—shoulders shaking, jaw clenched like he was embarrassed by the sound of it even as he couldn’t control it.

He tried to clamp down on it. Bit down on the inside of his cheek. Dragged in a breath through his nose like that might force the tears back where they belonged.

The next sob hit worse, deep, shuddering, violent enough that it folded him forward against her. His whole body shook with it, breath splintering into gasps he couldn’t regulate. He made a small, desperate sound in the back of his throat like he was furious at himself for losing control and powerless to stop it.

“I—” The word shattered the moment it left him.
He squeezed his eyes shut, tears spilling freely now, hot and relentless. His face pressed harder into her shoulder, as if hiding would somehow make it smaller.

“I don’t know how to do this,” he sobbed, the confession ripped out between uneven breaths. “I don’t know how to be with someone and not ruin it—”
