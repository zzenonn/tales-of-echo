Red-hot tears stung Sol’s eyes, burning away the beautiful facade he always wore. The careful composure, the smile polished for courts and companions alike, all of it melted under the weight of his father’s venomous words.

Sol sat staring at the parchment far longer than he should have, his father’s words burning into him like acid. He had read letters like this before, full of expectation and venom, but this one cut deeper, because for the first time, he’d let himself be open and honest. He had spoken of Adrien with warmth, and for that, his father had carved him down to nothing.

His eyes caught on the place where his father’s ink carved out the name Adrien, and he traced it once with his thumb, trembling. That single softness slipped into his letter had become the blade his father used to cut him down.

The tears broke then, falling heavily onto the parchment. His grief bled into the icy ink, warping the letters, smearing their cruelty into blurred shadows.

Abruptly, he shoved back his chair, boots striking too sharply against the stone as he stormed down the corridor. By the time he reached his room, the sobs broke loose; harsh, unsteady, audible, carrying the sound of someone who knew in his bones that his father would never truly understand or support him.

In his rush, the letter was left behind on the table, stained with blurred ink and salt. Its cruel words lay open in the glow of the lamplight; exposed and raw, waiting for someone else’s eyes to see what he could not bear to carry.

What Sol didn’t notice, as his sobs rattled the closed door to his room, was the soft slide of another envelope under the dorm’s front door. The paper was light and delicate, fragile in a way that made it feel almost out of place in the same room as the venomous ink left behind. Across the front, in graceful handwriting, it read: From Cassiopeia Lyra Aelia. His name was penned in that same gentle script, every curve of the letters carrying warmth his father’s hand never knew.

The seal gleamed in gold and silver wax, stamped with the crest of House Aelia: wings unfurled, circled with sun and moon blossoms. It rested quietly on the floor, waiting for someone to read it.

For once, he let himself just be; for once, he wasn’t an heir or son to be sharpened into a blade, he was just a friend.
