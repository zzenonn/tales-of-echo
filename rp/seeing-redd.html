Redd

The dormitory door shut behind them with a heavy, final thud.

Redd remained standing just inside the threshold, shoulders tight, breath still uneven from the confrontation. Sorjin’s words echoed in his head, sharp, defensive, wounded. The whole exchange had spiraled, tension layered over old fractures that had never quite healed.

He dragged a hand down his face.

They had tried.

He had tried.

That was the part that burned.

They had tried with Sorjin. Reached out. Apologized. Explained. Given space. Given patience. Again and again. Even when it would have been easier to walk away. Even when guilt had hollowed Redd out from the inside.

Yes, Redd had made his own mistakes. Many. He had hurt people, Sorjin definitely being on that list. He had mishandled things. He had failed.

And he had owned it.

He did not blame others. He didn't weaponize his pain. He didn't take the hurt done to him and decide it was justified hurting someone else in return.

Being hurt did not give you permission to hurt others.

Being a victim did not give you the right to create new ones.

Especially not dragging others who had nothing to do with this, like Griffin, into it.

Redd began pacing the common room, restless energy coiling through him.

“Unbelievable,” he muttered under his breath. “You don’t get to say you’re wounded and then swing at everyone in reach…”

His jaw tightened.

“I messed up. Fine. I own that. But I didn’t go after people who weren’t involved. I didn’t start taking collateral.” His voice sharpened, frustration bleeding through. “There’s a line.”

His gaze flicked first to Caspian, lingering a fraction longer, knowing the argument that would come.

“We need to stop coddling him,” he said, more controlled now, though the heat remained. “Every time he comes after us and flares up, we soften. We explain. We absorb it. At some point that stops being compassion and starts being permission.”

He exhaled slowly, forcing himself into something steadier. His posture straightened as his eyes moved to Vornakir, meeting that look squarely.

“We set boundaries,” Redd continued, voice cooling into strategy. “Clear ones. No more emotional gymnastics. No more bending ourselves in half to make sure no one feels discomfort.”

“If a spell duel is what he wants, then fine.”

His voice shifted then, no longer just angry, but resolute, edged with something dangerous.

“I’ll stand in the open and take the fireball if I have to. I won’t flinch. I won’t hide behind anyone. If he needs spectacle, I’ll give him spectacle.”

A faint, humorless curl touched his mouth.

“But he should understand something.”

Redd’s eyes cooled, steady and bright.

A pause.

“If he hurls fire at me, then he’d better be ready for the sky to answer back.”

His voice dropped, low and deliberate.

“Because when the moon comes down, it doesn’t miss.”

Silence hung there for a beat.

“I’m done chasing,” he finished quietly. Eyes briefly falling on Sunni. “If this is how he wants to resolve it, then tomorrow he gets exactly what he asked for.”

Redd did not interrupt him.

He stood there and took it.

Every word.

Every accusation.

The laugh. The inventory of sins. The tally sheet of damage.

His jaw tightened once, visibly, but he did not look away.

When Caspian finished, the silence that followed was thinner than glass.

Redd let out a short, incredulous laugh, sharp and hollow.

“Ha! A dozen?” he said, dry amusement threading through the anger. “That doesn’t even come close.”

The corner of his mouth twitched, not quite a smile, more fangs than warmth.

“Let’s not undersell my legacy.”

The joke was bitter, self-aware, cutting in two directions at once.

He inhaled slowly.

“You think I don’t know I’m walking collateral damage?” His voice was quieter now, but harder. “You think I don’t wake up aware of every crater I’ve left behind me?”

His gaze locked onto Caspian’s.

“I know exactly what I am.”

A beat.

He saw it in Sor’jin. The same flare. The same hunger to wound when wounded.

“It takes one to know one.”

There was no smile in it.

“You’re right. I’ve hurt people. I’ve roped people in. I’ve lashed out when I shouldn’t have.” His shoulders squared. “And when I did, you all told me. You dragged me back. You called me out. You made it clear where the line was.”

His voice sharpened.

“And I stopped.”

That hung there.

“I didn’t keep doing it. I didn’t double down. I didn’t glare at you like you’d betrayed me for daring to say no.”

His fingers flexed at his sides.

“You’re talking about compassion like I’m allergic to it. I’m not.” His eyes flicked briefly toward Sunni. “I know what patience looks like. I’ve been on the receiving end of it.”

Then his gaze moved to Vornakir.

“And don’t compare this to Scales.”

The name was deliberate.

“When Vornakir injured Kael, there was no malice in it. No resentment simmering underneath. It was power misjudged. It was ego. It was stupid.” A harsh breath. “But it wasn’t venom.”

His eyes hardened.

“Sor’jin was oozing it. Is still oozing in it. That’s the difference.”

He stepped forward half a pace, not aggressive, but planted.

“You say he hasn’t crossed the line again. Fine.”

His jaw tightened, something sharper flashing in his eyes.

“So what, we wait?” His voice rose, not shouting, but close. “We wait until he does? Until he covers the fields in flame? Until he forces us to—”

Redd cut himself off.

The words hung there anyway.

His mouth closed hard. His gaze dropped for half a second before lifting again, colder now.

“We don’t get infinite chances to react,” he said, quieter, but far more dangerous.

Redd’s voice lowered. His shoulders slumped.

It didn’t work; every sharp word Caspian and Redd slung at each other landed like a hammer behind his eyes.

He felt everything: Redd’s exhaustion, Caspian’s anger, the guilt, the pride. All of it tangled into a pressure in Sol’s head that made his vision blur at the edges.

He sucked in a shaky breath, then pushed himself to his feet, the chair scraping loudly against the floor. The look on his face could only be equated to a pissed-off mother who had to deal with quarreling children.

He looked between Redd and Caspian slowly and deliberately. “You’re both right,” Sol said, voice tight, “and you’re both wrong.” A long beat of silence stretched before he continued. “You know what the great thing about being alive is? The ability to disagree. Constantly. About big things, small things, what the right call is, and about stuff that’s inconsequential.” His hands curled tightly at his sides. “The good part is that disagreements are supposed to lead to new understanding and growth.” His gaze hardened. “I’m fine with occasional friendly disagreements and arguments, but what I will not tolerate is you two verbally assaulting each other.”

Sol turned and walked toward Redd, stopping directly in front of him, and looking him over head to toe.

“What you said to Caspian was an extremely low blow,” Sol said plainly. “And it was very unnecessary.”

Sol wasn’t yelling, which was unusual for him, especially when he got angry; this time, it had a frigid, matter-of-fact cadence to it that anyone present for his Sponsorship Day demonstration would’ve eerily reminded them of his father’s way of speaking.

He continued addressing Redd. “There was no reason to drag up his past like that when you know the consequences it led to, and especially when you know how hard he’s worked since then to be better.”

Sol’s tone softened slightly, not in a forgiving way, just honesty. “We’ve talked about your behavior before, and you and I both know you’re better than whatever this is.” He paused for a few seconds, then resumed, firmer. “You say you want better for yourself, and preach the benefit of tough love,” he said while holding his gaze. “So here’s my tough love. Then, without looking at either of them: “I think you two owe each other apologies, and not some half-assed, ‘sorry you’re offended’ nonsense.” He finally looked up. “Real ones.”

Silence settled again, and for a few seconds, he didn’t say anything. Then he exhaled a sharp, frustrated sigh, and the air around him rippled. A faint red haze pulsed outward from his body, and heat radiated outward, like anger was bleeding off him in waves before it could turn into something worse.
