Auri

The stone door of the tower whispered open, as though protesting the intrusion of any living thing. Darian Ashfall stepped inside first, boots stirring faint motes of dust that caught the violet light. Auriette followed half a step behind him, her small crimson fingers hooked tightly around his. She was no taller than Darian’s waist, with wavy dark hair and tiny horns peeking through her bangs. Her amber eyes glowed, bright and curious, and her tail swayed behind her with every step. She looked, Darian often thought, like a devil’s idea of a cherub.

Inside, the top floor of the tower shimmered with arcane secrets, as if the stones themselves remembered centuries of whispered spells. Floating lanterns drifted lazily across the room, illuminating the shelves heavy with ancient tomes. Beakers glowed with mysterious liquids, and mechanical instruments clicked and hummed in soft rhythms. Auri’s eyes widened, reflecting every spark of enchantment.

Artorius stood at the center of the room, motionless as carved stone. His skeletal face, polished smooth by centuries, was hidden partly beneath his hood, and the pale blue flames in his sockets flickered with quiet, watchful intelligence.

Auri stared up at him for a long, thoughtful stretch. Her tail slowly rose behind her, curling into the shape of a question mark.

“Darian,” she whispered far too loudly as she tugged at his robe with one hand and pointed with the other at Artorius. “He has a skeleton head.”

Darian’s mouth twitched, somewhere between mortified and endeared, before he pinched the bridge of his nose.

Artorius’ jaw clicked with amusement. “Yes,” he replied. “It appears I have misplaced the rest of me.” He lowered his head slightly. “Tell me, child— are you afraid?”

Auri looked to the ceiling in thought, then shook her head. “No.”

With that, she slipped free of Darian’s hand and darted deeper into the room. She pressed her nose to a beaker, poked a floating quill, tapped the cover of a book, and straightened a stack of papers neatly on the desk.

Darian watched her, the tension in his shoulders easing a little. “Well,” he murmured, “she’s settling in.”

“Fearless,” Artorius observed. The blue flames in his eyes softened, dimming at the edges— a skeletal equivalent of raised brows. “And…organized?”

“When the two meet,” Darian said, “I call that ‘Auri.’”

He stepped closer, lowering his voice. “Artorius…are you truly all right with this?”

Artorius didn’t answer immediately. His skull turned toward Auri as she stretched on her tiptoes toward a crystalline device humming with soft musical tones.

Only after watching her for a moment did he speak. “We have no other choice.”

Darian swallowed, jaw tightening. “After my delve, I’ll bring coin. Supplies. Enough to take care of her. And I’ll visit as often as I can.”

Auri rushed back then, arms full of stolen quills, a glowing orb, and a book. “Darian, are you leaving?” she asked, voice tiny.

Darian crouched to her level. “Just for a little while, Auri.”

Her face scrunched up in a brave, determined effort not to cry. Her nose wrinkled, and her tail drooped until the tip touched the floor. A little hiccup jumped in her throat, but she swallowed it fiercely, chin trembling once before she forced it still.

Darian brushed her cheek with his thumb. “I’ll be back before you know it.”

He pressed a kiss to the top of her head between her horns and stood. For a heartbeat, his hand hovered above her hair again— wanting to stay, needing to go. Auri carefully set down her treasures and waved both arms as he walked toward the door. Artorius lifted one skeletal hand in a slow, steady farewell.

The stone door closed with a resonant thrum.
Auri’s hands lowered. Her tail drooped weakly. “He left really fast…”

Artorius extended a hand. A pale spark bloomed above his palm. It swirled, brightened, and slowly unfolded into a flower of blue light. The petals gleamed like frost under moonbeams.

Auri’s breath caught. She stared in wondrous awe. “How did you make it?”

Artorius gestured to the library surrounding them. “These books hold every spell I have learned, have yet to learn, and will fail to learn. They can teach you anything you wish to know. If you like, I can read them to you.”

Her face lit up, the sadness beginning to dissolve into wonder. “Right now?”

“Right now,” Artorius said.

She spun toward the nearest stack of tomes. “Start with the really big one!”

Artorius followed her, and for the first time in centuries, the quiet tower felt more alive than it had ever been.

Ghost Bestie

The shadows danced lightly along the edges of Artorius’ study, as if the stones themselves were watching. The couch at its center was piled high with blankets and pillows arranged just the way Auri liked. There was no bed, as Artorius hadn’t needed one, and to this day, Auri still chose to sleep in the common area over her room in the dorm.

Artorius closed the old tome he had been reading from that evening and set Auri down gently. “Time to rest, little one,” he said, his voice like a calm ripple across still water.

Auri padded over to her nest and curled up among the pillows. Artorius tucked the blanket around her shoulders.

Just then, a sudden pop from beneath the couch made her jump. A tiny, translucent figure shot upward, spinning in the air with sparkling motes trailing behind it. Wispyr’s form was delicate, like a swirl of mist caught in the moonlight, with tiny flicking wings that hummed faintly. Their round, glowing eyes shone with endless curiosity, and a little curl of mist hinted at a mischievous smile that seemed to stretch impossibly wide as they twirled.

“Oh my gosh, hi, hi, hi! You’re so adorable! Your horns…an-and your tail! Wait, wait, wait— do you know about the secret passage under the library? Ahd ohhh have you met Pantrick yet?”

Auri froze, eyes wide, as the little being zipped and twirled, leaving a faint ghostly trail in the air.
Artorius’ flames flickered gently. “That is Wispyr. They…tend to know everything about everyone in this tower. They mean no harm.”

Auri blinked. “Okay…hello, Wispyr. I’m Auri.”

“Even your voice is adorable!” Wispyr squeaked, somersaulting over her head. “I know you saw the floating lanterns, but do you know about the book that glows when someone lies?”

Auri giggled, the nervous tension in her shoulders easing. “No…I didn’t know!”

A tall, broad figure the size of the doorway he walked through approached, apron dusted with flour and small glowing insects clinging to it. His eyes glimmered through shriveled sockets, mouth curved in a permanent gentle grin. “It’s time for Auri to rest, Wispyr. Unlike the rest of us, she is still alive, so she needs to sleep,” he said in a low, rumbling voice.

“Mmmph, fiiiinnneee,” Wispyr pouted, but they didn’t protest.

Pantrick held out a steaming cup, swirling with purple and blue that tickled Auri’s nose when she inhaled. Hesitant, she sipped, and a warm sigh escaped her lips. She drank the rest of the mysterious liquid and nestled back into the couch cushions.

Wispyr zipped around her, chattering nonstop. “Oh! When you wake up, I’ll show you that hidden passage I told you about! And we can try some of Panrick’s cookies! He hasn’t made any since we were alive, but I bet he still remembers how, right, Pantrick?”

Auri giggled, watching Wispyr float in the air above her. “Okay, that sounds like fun.”

The little ghost settled onto the couch beside her, determined to stay by her side until she woke up so they could play, buzzing lightly like a warm, tiny cloud, and Pantrick disappeared back toward the kitchen with a nod.

“Goodnight, everyone,” Auri whispered, a tiny smile curling across her face.
