The Resident Therapist

The Spark Saga · Sol/Sunni Redd
November 28, 2025 – November 30, 2025
Sol/Sunni

Sol sat curled into the far corner of the common room sofa, one leg folded beneath him and the other pulled close, a heavy sketchbook balanced across his knees. The fire in the hearth burned low and warm across the room, casting an amber glow on the walls and setting quiet shadows to dance along the shelves.

He had a soft, distracted smile plastered across his face, which he often wore when his thoughts drifted elsewhere.

The pages beneath his pencil were alive with colorful lines. He was drawing mock-up panels for yet another outfit; he was currently working on a dress shirt with glowing threads meant to echo ecto in motion. With careful strokes of his pencil, he subtly worked arcane glyph symbols into shirt cuffs.

Sol paused, studying it. “Hmm,” he murmured. “It’s not right yet, Adrien said he listens to ecto like it’s a voice in his head. How the hell do I translate that to clothing?!”

He added faint accents of luminous purple and gold along the collar, then frowned. “No, that’s loud,” he decided. “He’a not really an attract-the-crowd kinda person.”

Sol turned the page and began a new sketch. He didn’t look up from his page even when he became quietly aware that someone was nearby.

“Hey,” he said while drawing the basics of a figure on the page. “I know you’re there, your aura is brighter than the surface of the sun,” he jested. “You can come in. I’m not doing anything that important right now.”

Redd

Redd slipped into the common room like someone hoping the shadows would swallow him. His hair looked like he’d run a hand through it one too many times; his shirt was wrinkled, one corner of the collar bent inward, and he moved with the loose, slumped exhaustion of someone running on fumes.

He noticed Sol before he said anything—the sketchbook, the glowing threadwork, the glyphs taking shape. A tiny, tired smile tugged at the corner of his mouth.

“…You’re getting stupidly good at that,” he murmured as he approached. “I'm keeping the sponsorship day outfit you made for me.”
It came out soft, almost shy—not Redd’s usual armor.

He sank onto the opposite end of the couch with a slow exhale, elbows on his knees, hands dangling loosely. For a moment he just sat there, staring at the floorboards as if waiting for the room to do him the courtesy of swallowing him whole.

When he finally spoke again, it was strained around the edges.

“I’m fine,” he started—and immediately winced, running a hand over his face. “No. No, that’s bullshit. I’m not… fine.”

He hesitated, breathing out through his nose. “It’s about Zurr’ka.”

His voice thinned, unraveling.

“I spent the whole night before our duel practicing. Actual drills. Footwork. Spell transitions. Stuff I haven’t done that seriously in years.” He huffed a humorless laugh. “And it wasn’t even about winning. I just… didn’t want to let him down. Didn’t want him to think I wasn’t taking him seriously.”

He swallowed, jaw tightening.

“And then he canceled. And something in me just—broke. Quietly. Neatly. Like a little switch flipped.” He tapped his temple once. “Right here. Off.”

His shoulders crept higher, tension coiling in them.

“And instead of dealing with it, I went and flirted with Klath.”
A bitter scoff. “Because apparently when I actually care about someone, my brain decides the best move is self-destruction.”

He looked away.

“And then when Zurr’ka fought Diana—when he actually needed people there—I wasn’t with him, I wasn’t even with you, or Vornakir, or Caspian, or Auri. I was sitting with Zurr’ka’s family. Because Klath dragged me into that seat and I didn’t know how to say no without making it worse.”

His voice cracked on the next line.

“And I hated myself the entire time.”

He dragged in a shaky breath.

“And Klath…” Redd’s voice softened, almost embarrassed. “He wasn’t awful about it. He wasn’t even annoyed. He just… put the pieces together faster than I could hide them, I guess.”

Redd’s gaze drifted downward, lashes low.

“And then the duel.”

He paused, jaw working.

“That fire wasn’t like anything I’ve faced before. It was huge. Loud. Alive in a way that made every bit of me go tight. I didn’t think I could handle it. I didn’t think I even should.”
His breath caught, thin. “For a second… I just didn’t want to try.”

His hands curled into loose fists against his knees.

“On the inside? I just wanted it to be over. All of it. The embarrassment, the shame, the stupid spiral I put myself in. That fire felt like a way out, and that thought scares the hell out of me.”

He swallowed hard, shoulders folding in.

“It wasn’t Klath. It wasn’t even the duel itself. It was everything piling up. Me realizing how badly I’d messed things up before Zurr’ka even stepped into the arena.”

Silence settled heavy between them.

Finally, he whispered, “Everyone was there. Everyone saw everything. And I just— I keep thinking there’s something wrong with me. Like I ruin things even when I’m trying so hard not to.”

He lifted his eyes—not fully to Sol’s, but close, like he was afraid of what real eye contact would show.

“I don’t know how to fix it,” he said softly. “I don’t know how to fix me.”

He leaned back, exhaustion dragging every line of him downward.

“I’m so tired.”

And then he fell quiet, the hush of the room swallowing the last of his words, waiting for Sol to respond—or simply sit with him in the silence.

Sol/Sunni

Sol didn’t interrupt him. He listened, leaning forward with his elbows on his knees, fingers laced together, and eyes steady on Redd the whole time. When Redd finally trailed off into that quiet, hollow “I’m so tired,” Sol didn’t immediately rush in to fill the silence. He let his friend breathe and simmer with his thoughts before he spoke.

Sol carefully closed his sketchbook and set it aside on the table. Then he stood, crossed the space between them, and snapped his fingers.

Light unfurled around Redd like a slow exhale. Threads of soft, silver magic shimmered through the air, smoothing his rumpled collar and coaxing the wrinkles out of his shirt. A trail of golden magic threaded its way through Redd’s hair, gently shifting it into how he normally styled it.

Sol tilted his head, offering his friend a small smile. “First of all,” he said gently, “you can’t feel good if you don’t look good.”

The corner of his mouth curled up. “And secondly, you can keep the Sponsorship Day outfit. I had absolutely no intention of taking it back.”

His humor softened into compassion as Sol sat back down beside him. “Redd, none of what you just told me makes you weak, or selfish, or broken.” His voice was low and steady. “It makes you someone who’s cared too much for a second longer than it was safe to.”

He took a breath in through his nose, then out slowly. “When Adrien and I fight,” Sol continued after a moment, eyes dropping briefly before lifting again, “and yes, believe it or not, it does happen, we made a rule early on.”

He faintly smiled. “We give each other 24 hours alone to sulk, spiral, cool off, and process everything.” He glanced back at Redd. “And afterwards, no matter how awkward it is, no matter how scared we are that we’ll say the wrong thing, no matter how mad we might still feel, we sit down and talk. And I’m not talking about talking just to be right and win a fight, but to have an open and honest conversation about our feelings and what’s going on in our heads.”

Sol leaned forward a little. “Communication isn’t about having the perfect words; it doesn’t need to be pretty, it just needs to be honest.”

A beat passed.

“The hardest step in reconciliation is always the first one,” he admitted softly. “Walking up to someone you care about and telling them how you feel.“ Sol reached out and held his friend's hand. “Silence leads to miscommunication and misinterpretations, and that kills relations more than words ever could, Redd.”

His eyes softened. “And you?” Sol shook his head. “You don’t need to be fixed. That would mean you’re broken.” A quiet certainty crept into his voice. “And you’re not broken. You just move too fast when your emotions get loud; you leap before you think.”

He gestured lightly toward Redd’s chest. “So maybe that’s the ticket. You don’t need to change who you are, you just have to learn to stop for half a second, sit with your feelings, name them, and then decide what you want to do with them.”

Sol exhaled. “You don’t ruin things,” he said. “You panic when you don’t want to lose them. Those aren’t the same thing.”

Another pause filled the room.

“You don’t have to carry this alone,” he said. “Not with me here.” Sol leaned back on the couch. “So let’s take this one step at a time.”

He glanced sideways at Redd. “When Zurr’ka told you he didn’t want to duel with you on Sponsorship Day, what went through your head? How did it make you feel? It doesn’t matter how stupid or childish you think it sounds, just say it out loud.” There was a hint of maternal maturity in his voice, a trait he no doubt acquired from his mother.

Redd

Redd’s fingers tightened minutely around Sol’s, like his hand had made the decision before the rest of him could. The magic that smoothed his shirt and coaxed his hair back into place earned a small, weak breath that might’ve been a laugh on a better day. Tonight it just made his shoulders drop a fraction, as if being put back together on the outside made it easier to try on the inside.

When Sol asked the question, the one he’d been circling around without touching, Redd went still.

His eyes flicked away. His throat worked once. He breathed in—shallow—and let it out slow, trying not to run from the moment.

“Okay,” he murmured, almost to himself. “Just… feelings.”

It took him a second to find his voice.

“When he canceled,” Redd said quietly, “the first thing I felt was… hurt. Like really hurt. I know it wasn’t supposed to be a big deal, but it… it hit me. Hard.”
His brow furrowed, the memory still sharp. “I’d actually tried. I put work into becoming a top ranked spell duelist. Actually felt good about myself for once. And it felt like all of that just— evaporated.”

He swallowed, staring at some point on the floor.

“And then I got scared.”
The admission came out softer, almost ashamed. “Not of him. Of… becoming the next Sorjin. Of being seen as weak, or like I wasn’t worth the trouble. It was just a flash, but it was there.”

His voice wavered.

“And it felt like rejection. Even though I know it wasn’t meant like that. In the moment, it just—” He shook his head once. “It felt like he didn’t want me. Like I wasn’t… important.”

Redd inhaled sharply, as if about to explain himself, or apologize, or backtrack into some spiral he’d been going around the last 48 hours. But he caught it—actually caught it—and stopped. His lips pressed together. He closed his eyes for a second and breathed through it.

“You said to start with the feelings,” he murmured. “So… those are the feelings.”

He opened his eyes again, turning them back toward Sol—tentative, uncertain, searching for whether he’d done it right.

“I’m… trying.”

Sol/Sunni

Sol watched as Redd spoke; when he finished, Sol nodded. “It’s a good start,” he said gently. “That’s exactly how you do it, name the feelings instead of running from them.”

He lifted their hands, just enough to give an affirming squeeze, before he released them and stood up. With a soft flick of his fingers, an illusion bloomed in the air between them. A copy of Redd took shape in pale, gold silver. Around the illusion, an aura began to shimmer to life.

Muted gold flickered at the chest: yearning. Deep ember-orange at the ribs: hurt. Cold blue threaded the edges: fear. A Soft green pooled low by the belly: hope. The colors swirled and faded in and out with a quiet rhythm.

Sol gestured to it. “This is your emotional aura right now, post-duel. As you can see, you’re very nuanced and are feeling so many different things at once.”

Then, with another motion of his hand, the illusion shifted, and Zurr’ka appeared instead.

“Now, I want you to try something new.” He stepped to the side so Redd had an unobstructed view of Zurr’ka. “Put yourself where he was. Right before the fight. Imagine what he might have felt when he told you he couldn’t duel alongside you.”

Sol invited Redd to think deeply. “Not only is he a strong, proud, and skilled duelist, but he’s shown to be protective of you in the past, so that he wouldn’t make that decision lightly.”

“So ask yourself, what does it cost someone like that to admit they can’t show up as expected?”

He glanced at Redd. “What do you think he felt telling you he didn’t want the duel?” A beat of silence paused the conversation. Then Sol asked the knife-turning question carefully.

“Now, stay in his shoes,” Sol said gently. “Imagine you’re already afraid you’re disappointing someone you care about, and then you see them flirting with your brother.”

He let the words sit for a moment so Redd could digest them. “How do you think that felt from there?” Sol asked. “And why do you think it would hurt the way it did?”

His voice softened, layered with empathy on both sides. “This is all about perspective, Redd.” He gave him a sympathetic smile. “Hurt bends in different directions depending on where you’re standing.”

Sol stepped closer to the illusion of Zurr’ka and placed his palm near where a heart would be. “So tell me, Redd, what do you think was going through Zurr’ka’s mind?”

Redd

Redd stared at the illusion as it shifted, his own outline dissolving into the broad silhouette of Zurr’ka rendered in deep ember-red light. Ash-gray shadows clung to the edges like weight made visible. A pulse of faint ruby glowed where a heart would be.

Seeing him like that—quiet, stripped of armor and posture—pulled something tight in Redd’s chest.

He inhaled slow, then let the breath tremble out.
“…Okay,” he murmured, almost to himself. “If I put myself where he was…”

He studied the illusion’s ember-lit ribs, the pulse at its center, and tried to follow the thread Sol had offered him.

“He was under pressure. Real pressure. Sponsorship Day, his family watching his every move…” Redd’s mouth tightened. “It’s always there for him, but that day it must’ve been—worse.”

His jaw tightened. “He doesn’t talk about it, but you can see it. It’s always there in the background.”

Redd’s gaze flicked to the ash-grey haze clinging to the illusion’s outline.

“And after losing to Diana… gods.”
He swallowed, throat tight.
“That had to crush him. Not just pride—his whole foundation. He’s one of the strongest duelists in the academy. Losing like that, in front of everyone? In front of his family?”
Redd exhaled, slow and pained. “That would break anyone.”

He shifted, elbows dropping to his knees.

“And then there’s me.”

The words came out small.

“And I think he was scared of dragging me into it.”
Another slow breath.
“He knows what his family is like. He knows exactly what they can do. Walking into the arena with me wouldn’t have been just a duel. It would’ve been an announcement. An introduction.”

His voice lowered.
“And… after Sorjin? I think he was terrified of repeating that. Of putting me in the same kind of situation. Of… letting me get hurt.”

Redd’s gaze lingered on the ash-gray shimmer around the illusion’s edges.

“And the thing with Klath—”
He winced, as if the memory still had teeth.
“Zurr’ka probably knew I wasn’t serious. But that doesn’t mean it didn’t scare him. Not because of me and Klath, but because of what his family could twist it into. What they could do with it. How it might make everything worse.”

A moment passes. Then, softer:

“I think he felt caught. Like he wanted me there, but didn’t want me anywhere near that fire.”

He looked down, shoulders curling inward.
For a moment, his breath stuttered—an old habit, the instinctive tumble toward I made everything worse, I pushed him, I ruined it—

—but he squeezed his eyes shut, stopped himself, and breathed.

He opened his eyes again, looking at the ember-red illusion with something soft and aching.

When he spoke again, his voice was quieter but steadier.

“So… yeah,” he finished, barely above a whisper. “I think he was scared. Not of me. For me.”

And Redd stayed there, grounded but fragile, refusing the familiar spiral as he waited for Sol to guide him to the next step.

Sol/Sunni

Sol watched Redd the same way he’d listened earlier: quiet and intent, eyes shining with pride. When Redd finished, Sol smiled.

“That,” he said softly, “was empathy.”

With a slight flick of his fingers, the ember-lit illusion shimmered and dissolved into golden dust that faded into the firelight. Sol settled back onto the couch beside Redd.

“I’m really proud of you,” he added. “You could’ve stayed angry and hurt, but instead you chose to understand.”

His voice lowered, enough to make the next part feel important instead of just heavy.

“Okay,” Sol said. “Now the scary part.” He turned toward Redd. “With everything you know now: about him, about yourself. What would you say to Zurr’ka?”

“Don’t do a polished ‘I should say this’ version,” Sol added gently. “Just the truth.”

He paused. “If he were sitting right here,” Sol said, gesturing next to Redd, “what would your heart say?”

Sol quieted, letting Redd leap on his own.

Redd

Redd stared at the empty space beside him for a long moment, the air still warm from where Sol had motioned. His expression didn’t crumble this time—didn’t spiral. It just settled, like something inside him finally clicked into place.

He drew in a slow breath.

“…I think I get it now,” he said quietly.

His eyes lowered to his hands, but there was no shame in the gesture—only thoughtfulness. “Everything I’m realizing right now… everything I’m feeling…” He shook his head once, almost ruefully. “None of it matters unless I say it to him.”

Redd leaned back slightly, shoulders loosening with the weight of the truth.

A faint, steadying breath.

“It’s his to hear,” Redd murmured. “And his to answer.”

He didn’t rise. He didn’t rush off. He just sat there, holding the realization like something fragile and solid in his hands.

Sol/Sunni

Sol leaned back against the couch, arms folding loosely across his chest. He flashed Redd an all-knowing smile.

“Well,” he said, amusement layered with sincerity.

“What the hell are you still doing sitting here talking to me?”

His eyes shone with encouragement. “Go, before you overthink your way back into silence.”
