The hallway outside Redd’s door was quiet, the kind of late-evening quiet that made even small sounds feel louder than they should. Auri stood there for a moment, shifting her weight from one foot to the other as she looked at the familiar door. Normally, she would have knocked immediately. It was, after all, time for their weekly sleepover. Something she looked forward to all week.

Tonight was…different.

She adjusted the strap of the canvas bag resting against her hip, glancing down at it like it might offer reassurance. Inside were the usual things she always brought: books she probably wouldn’t end up reading, an insulated, metal container filled with hot tea, and some of his favorite snacks she had picked up earlier that evening. A peace offering, even if she wasn’t going to call it that out loud. 

Draped over her arm was her soft, oversized blanket she insisted was superior to the one Redd insisted was “perfectly adequate.” She brought it every time, and every time she reminded him it was time to break out the “good blanket.”

It was part of what had become a ritual. 

Auri tugged absently at the sleeve of her pajamas as she waited there another second. The little frog pattern scattered across the fabric made the whole outfit look almost comically cozy. The pants were slightly too long on her, bunching around her ankles, and the oversized band tee hung comfortably over her frame. Her hair was tied up in a knot, bangs held back with a matching green headband. She had dressed like she always did for their sleepovers. Like tonight was normal, even though it wasn’t.

Her tail flicked once behind her, betraying the nervous energy she was trying very hard not to show. For a brief moment, her gaze dropped to the floor. So much had happened since the last time they had spoken. The letter he’d written flicked through her mind.

*I understand why you did it. It didn’t help.*

Her chest tightened slightly. Auri inhaled slowly, steadying herself, and lifted her hand and knocked. For a second she simply waited, shifting the blanket higher on her arm as the quiet settled back around her. Then she called through the door, her voice warm and carefully light. 

“...Hi, Redd, are you in there?”

### &#x270D; Redd

The knock landed softly against the wood.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then there was the faint scrape of a chair shifting inside the room, followed by the quiet thud of something being nudged out of the way. A second later came the soft flutter of wings as Whimsy lifted from somewhere inside with an indignant little rustle, as if disturbed from a perfectly good perch.

The latch turned.

When the door opened, Redd stood there leaning slightly against the frame, one hand still resting on the handle. He looked…tired. Not the theatrical kind he sometimes wore like a costume, but the sort that settled in the eyes and softened the sharpness he usually carried.

His hair was a little messier than usual, like he’d run a hand through it too many times. The faintest shadow of bruising still lingered near his collarbone where his shirt dipped open at the throat.

For a brief second, he just looked at her.

Then his gaze dropped to the blanket.

Then the bag.

Then the frog pajamas.

A corner of his mouth lifted — small, but genuine.

“You brought the good blanket,” he said, voice low with quiet amusement.

He shifted his weight, stepping back from the doorway and pushing it open wider without hesitation, already making space for her to come in.

“Caspian tried to steal the last of the snacks earlier,” he added casually, glancing toward the room behind him. “So if you’ve got contraband in that bag you should probably declare it now before I’m forced to search you.”

The joke was familiar. Ritual.

But as he looked back at her again, the humor softened a little, something quieter settling behind it. His tone gentled.

“…Hey, Auri.”

He didn’t mention Zurr’ka.

Not yet.

Instead he tipped his head slightly toward the inside of the room.

“You coming in, or are you planning to make the hallway your new sleepover spot?”

Because you don’t deserve to feel like that. Then I saw him that day on the bench, and I…” she exalted softly. “I thought maybe if I understood what was happening from his side, I could…help. Or at least let go of that anger. Make sense of it.” 

“But none of that makes it the right call.” 

A small, self-aware breath escaped her, and her tail curled loosely around her ankle beneath the blanket. 

“I read your letter,” she added more quietly. “And you’re right. What I did didn’t help.” 

There was no defensiveness in her words. Just acceptance, and something more vulnerable slipping through. 

“I’m so sorry, Redd. I don’t want to be someone who makes things harder for you when you’re already struggling. I wanted to show up for you,” she finished gently. “But I chose the wrong way to do it.” 

She let her words settle in the air, giving him the space to decide what he wanted or needed from her.
