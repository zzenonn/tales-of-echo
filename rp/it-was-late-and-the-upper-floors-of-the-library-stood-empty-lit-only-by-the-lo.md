### &#x270D; Auri
<sub>Dec 17, 2025 &middot; 05:27 AM UTC</sub>

It was late, and the upper floors of the library stood empty, lit only by the low glow of mana-lamps. Not a sound could be heard aside from the distant creak of old shelves settling in the quiet and the soft rustle of paper. Auri sat at one of the long oak tables near the windows, her spellbook open before her. 

Auri’s hair was pulled into a braid to keep it out of her face, her wispy bangs framing her face. Even in her study clothes, there was a sense of purpose in the way she held herself. 

She wrote slowly and deliberately. Not copying from any text, but refining— lines of script interwoven with diagrams, ecto-flow arrows, marginal corrections layered over older notes. This was where she thought best: ink following thought, thought following pattern.

Sol was meant to meet with her here, and she had arrived early, as was her habit. Apparently, there was a particular spell he wanted to work on together. He hadn’t said which, only that it mattered to him, and that alone had been enough to pique her curiosity.

Auri glanced toward the library entrance, then returned to her work, adding a quiet annotation in the margin. 

Whatever he wanted to work on, she was ready.

### &#x270D; Sol/Sunni
<sub>Dec 18, 2025 &middot; 09:07 PM UTC</sub>

Sol paused before entering the upper stacks of the library, letting the hush of the library settle around him. For a moment, he watched Auri from a distance, noting the careful movement of her pen and the way her posture never slackened.

He was thinking about how combat had ways of stripping things down to the simplest truths, and the truth he’d been circling since the Quasi was an ugly one. Sol was good at ending fights without force: shaping convincing illusions, using a silver tongue sharpened by enchantment, creating calm where there should’ve been panic. He could turn enemies against one another, soften hearts, bolster allies, and keep people standing when they should’ve fallen. But when it came time to *break* something, when the lich had stood there, unmoved by charm or fear, immune to persuasion and untouched by illusion, he’d been useless.

Sol exhaled and crossed the room. The chair across from Auri scraped softly as he pulled it out and sat down. For a second, he said nothing. Instead, he placed his spellbook on the table between them and pushed it forward.

The pages were already open to a page with clean, deliberate script, structural notes in the margins, and arcane formulae arranged like architecture.

*Shatter.*

Sol rested his palm on the book. “I don’t know any combat spells that actually *hit* my opponent,” he said, not quite looking at her. “Not ones that matter anyway.”

He swallowed, then lifted his gaze. “I can talk people down. Trick them. Make them feel things they don’t want to feel, see things they don’t want to see.” A longer pause filled the air. “But in the Quasi, none of that mattered. I couldn’t touch her, I couldn’t stop her, I couldn’t even slow her down.”

His thumb traced the edge of the page, stopping just beneath the spell’s sigil. “I felt helpless,” Sol admitted, voice bare. “And I don’t ever want to feel that way again.”

He finally met Auri’s eyes, resolve settling in his shoulders like a decision already made.

“I need you to teach me this.”

“I’m glad you told me,” she said softly, taking his hands in hers if he allowed it.

Auri lifted her tea, drained it, and set the empty cup on the table.

“Artorius used to say,” she murmured wistfully, as if the man’s words were law itself, her affection clear as it always was when she spoke of him, “that everything has a weak point. You simply have to pay close enough attention to see where it *wants* to break.”

“It’s like when you cast *Vicious Mockery*, it’s more effective when you know what breaks someone. An insecurity, or a phobia. It’s just more…structural than mental.”

She nudged the cup toward Sol. “With *Shatter*, you focus the sound waves where the structure is already strained, and let the frequency do the rest.”

“So?” She lifted her eyebrows in quiet amusement, her tail giving a small, thoughtful flick behind her. “Where does it want to break?”

Sol couldn’t help the smile that tugged at his mouth when she mentioned the bladesong.

“Guess we’re even, then,” he said warmly. “I help you survive a lich and teach you creative use of your magic, and you help me learn magical theory and how to blow stuff up.” Sol laughed way louder than would be acceptable in a library setting.

At the mention of *Hypnotic Pattern*, he let out a soft huff, half-amused, half-resigned. “There’s been a noticeable trend lately. We’ve been fighting big, dramatic bosses with egos the size of towers and minds ironclad against my charming ways.” He shook his head. “As much as I love illusions, enchantments, and playing mental chess, I’d really like a backup plan for when that fails.”

His gaze lifted back to her, earnest taking hold in his expression. “So, thank you, really, for teaching me.”

When she nudged the cup toward him, Sol straightened, focus sharpening. He picked it up carefully and turned it in his hands, eyes tracing its surface, looking for any weak points. He slowed his breathing and let the library’s quiet hum sink in: the faint buzz of the lamps, the silent whispers of fellow students, the soft, ever-present ambient magic, resonant sounds that most people never noticed.

There. He had found it: a barely visible flaw, a tiny scratch along the base of the cup.

“That,” Sol said, more to himself than to Auri.

He closed his eyes and reached outward, feeling for the ambient sound in the room, feeling for the way vibrations layered and overlapped like threads in a tapestry. He guided them gently, coaxing them toward the scratch, narrowing the focus, tightening the—

The library doors suddenly opened behind him. Voices, laughter, and footsteps echoed just a little too sharply in the quiet space. Sol’s concentration splintered, and the vibrational resonance slipped.

***BOOM.***

The sound detonated behind him like a pack of explosives. A thunderous crack tore through the quiet upper floor as a nearby shelf exploded outward in a mess of chaos. Books flew in every direction, bindings slamming against tables and the floor in a cascading avalanche of paper and leather. The nearby lamps flickered violently before stabilizing, their glow suddenly much dimmer in the aftermath. Dead quiet crashed through the library a second later.

Sol froze. Slowly and painfully, he turned his head just enough to see the mess he made.

His ears started ringing. “Oh gods,” he whispered.

His face went crimson in an instant: ears, cheeks, neck, all of it. He dropped back into his chair like it might swallow him whole and brought both hands up to cover his face.

“I—” He groaned softly into his palms. “I am *so* sorry.”

Somewhere between mortification and panic, he sank lower in his seat, shoulders hunching inward.

“I swear,” he muffled, “I was aiming for the cup.”

Her tail snapped rigid behind her as instinct took over, shoulders squaring as the shelf shattered apart in a violent spray of wood and dust. All she had instructed him to do was *find* the weak point, but in the chaos, the distinction hardly mattered at the moment.

The couple stood frozen, staring at the wreckage. So did Auri.

The splintered shelf, the books scattered across the floor, the dust drifting lazily through the light, undecided about where to settle.

She swallowed. “It…it’s okay,” she said quietly, more to herself than anyone else. One hand lifted, palm outward. “Let’s not panic.”

“Now that that’s taken care of,” she said decidedly, “There’s only one thing left to do.”

She caught Sol’s hand firmly and immediately pulled him into motion if he allowed it, boots skidding slightly on the polished floor as she led them swiftly between the stacks.

Unless he stopped her, Auri continued until they were well outside, the cool night air hitting her flushed crimson skin. Only then did she slow, releasing his hand and bending forward slightly to catch her breath. 

Then she laughed. 

It burst out of her, bright and uncontrollable, as she dropped onto the soft grass of the training field, tail thrashing freely now as she topped back onto her elbows. 

“You know,” she said between fits of giggling, “I don’t even know where we’re going.” She waved one hand vaguely, then put her hands over her face. “Did you *see* their faces?” 

She glanced back toward the library, still grinning. “I bet they were trying to find a quiet place to--- and then--- “ she broke off, laughing harder. “Then you blew up the library. Oh my gods…”
