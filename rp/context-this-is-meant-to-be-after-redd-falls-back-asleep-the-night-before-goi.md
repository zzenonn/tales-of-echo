The night was alive with monsters. Magma-born beasts stalked the streets of Luminar, their hulking bodies split with glowing seams, dripping fire that hissed as it scorched the cobblestones. Every step they took left molten pits in the earth; every roar sent sparks scattering like arrows loosed from the dark. Villagers fled in blind terror, their cries ripped away by the heat, but the creatures pursued without mercy, tearing through homes and flesh with the same burning hunger.

Redd ran. He could feel the suffocating heat claw at his lungs, every breath of smoke cutting deeper, but his legs carried him faster, faster, though never fast enough. His title thundered in his head with every step: lord, lord, lord. He should have been leading, defending, saving. Instead, he was fleeing through streets he once claimed as his own. His hands were empty; no spell rising to his lips, no wild strength at his back; just sweat and ash slipping down his skin.

One of the magma beasts lurched past him, a living inferno dragging its claw through a stone wall as if it were parchment. Another clambered onto a roof, the weight collapsing the beams in a spray of embers. Neighbors’ faces blurred in the chaos: a guard he once had a dalliance with, engulfed in molten claws; a mother clutching her child as fire swallowed them whole. Their screams rose, then vanished, leaving only the hunt.

And then he saw them.

His father’s form loomed ahead, face lit in ghastly glow, features carved with disappointment. The voice came without lips moving, deep and thunderous: “You were never enough. You shame this family. You shame your name.”

Beside him, Bluemond—Blue—stood small and fragile in the inferno’s glow. Barely twelve, his eyes were wide with terror, his hand outstretched. “Redd!” he cried, voice cracking. “Why did you leave me?”

Redd’s chest clenched. He reached, desperate, his arm trembling toward that hand. “I didn’t want to!” His voice broke, swallowed by the roar of flame and beasts. The magma creatures prowled between them, each step collapsing the ground into pits of molten stone, their burning eyes fixed on him. His father’s shadow grew taller, darker, stretching across the fire, while Blue’s hand slipped further from reach.

And still the fire closed in, the roar of the beasts swallowing his voice before he could speak again.

The hand on his shoulder made him flinch. It should have been another claw, another lash of fire—but it wasn’t. Coolness spilled through him, steady as rain. He turned, and through the haze and smoke, he saw her.

Zašia.

At first she looked as he knew her: calm, steady, her presence like an anchor dropped into the churning sea of nightmare. Her eyes held him with a clarity that cut through the smoke, her form untouched by the blaze that devoured everything else. For a heartbeat, he almost believed it. That she could be here. That she could be real. That maybe he wasn’t alone.

But then his vision wavered. The fire warped, or maybe he did. Her outline split and fractured—layer upon layer of Zašia overlapping, crowding each other, each one different. One serene, gaze heavy with compassion. One wrathful, lips curved into a snarl. Another weeping, another smiling too wide, another cloaked in silence. They flickered like shards of glass catching light, a dozen facets of her compressed into one impossible figure.

And somehow, impossibly, it still fit. Like this was the truth of her now. A truth too great for him to bear.

Redd’s throat went dry. The first impression of comfort curdled into something else, not fear, not horror—something worse. Awe. Painful awe. Because she was beautiful. Because she was loyal. Because she was here, reaching through the fire when no one else could. Because she had always been more—stronger, steadier, sharper—while he was nothing but ash and ruin. She was everything he wasn’t, and everything he could never deserve.

He staggered back a step, smoke catching in his lungs. His eyes burned, though whether from heat or shame he could not tell. “No…” The word rasped out of him, weak, cracked. “No, don’t—don’t do this.” His voice rose, ragged with panic. “I don’t deserve it. I don’t deserve you, Zurr'ka, Caspian, Vornakir, Sol. I don't deserve anyone!”

The words tumbled raw, broken, desperate: “Go away. Don’t follow me. I’ll only drag you down. Everything I touch, I ruin. Everything I try to hold, I break.”

The fire roared back, as if answering his confession. Blue’s voice echoed from somewhere ahead, still pleading, still reaching. His father’s shadow stretched longer, darker. The magma beasts circled, molten jaws slavering for the hunt.

And Redd did the only thing he knew how to do. He ran.

Then he turned. The village stretched out in all directions, burning hotter the deeper he went. To his left, rows of timber houses collapsed one after the other, sparks spiraling upward like dying prayers. To his right, alleys choked with smoke and ash, fire-beasts prowling in the glow. Overhead, rafters and beams cracked apart, tumbling in showers of cinders. He ran through it all, turning left and right and up again, plunging deeper into the furnace-heart of Luminar. Each step drew him further into the inferno, heat pressing closer, closer, as if the village itself wanted to consume him whole.

But still he ran.

And somewhere behind him—steady, impossible, unyielding—her presence lingered. Urging him to fight.

Zašia’s words cut through the blaze like cold steel through molten glass. For the first time, they didn’t just echo—they anchored. The storm of heat and guilt that had smothered him paused, just for a breath, and something inside Redd remembered how to move.

He turned toward the nearest magma beast. It lunged, molten claws carving through the air, but he met it halfway. His eyes flashed gold, the slit-pupil glint of something not entirely human. Scales shimmered faintly along his jaw and forearms, his hands curving into claws that gleamed silver-white in the firelight. The beast struck—Redd met it with a hiss.

Moonlight broke through the smoke. It shouldn’t have been possible in this inferno, but it found him anyway, pouring down in a silvery shaft that split the dark. The spell ignited as it touched him, a vertical column of pale radiance that sliced through the creature’s molten chest. The beast screamed, body hardening to obsidian before collapsing into shards.

Another came. Redd moved like a storm—half man, half serpent, half something older than both. His movements blurred, driven by instinct more than form. When he swung, his claws left streaks of radiant light; when he breathed, smoke parted in obedience. For the first time in the dream, the fire yielded.

He stood tall in the glow, chest heaving, the Moonbeam’s silver flame still burning faintly around his form. Zašia’s voice echoed through the chaos, steady, unrelenting: You are better than this.

For a heartbeat, he almost believed her. The village no longer looked like a graveyard—it looked winnable. The beasts faltered. The inferno retreated an inch. His heartbeat thundered in rhythm with the rain she’d called down. Maybe he could save this place. Maybe he wasn’t a failure after all.

Then the heat changed.

It pressed inward—focused, deliberate, as though something greater than the beasts had begun to watch. The ground trembled. The air thickened with the taste of metal and ash. The flames along the street drew together, swirling into a single column of molten light. The magma creatures lowered their heads, silent and reverent.

From that blaze stepped a figure.

At first, Redd braced himself for another monster. A final creature to teardown to win the day. But then he saw the face.

His own.

The apparition stepped from the inferno, shoulders squared and posture dripping with lazy confidence. Fire clung to him like perfume, coiling in elegant ribbons that refused to die. His grin was sharp and knowing.

He looked good, and that was the worst part. The flames sculpted him in impossible perfection—the smirk that had once charmed half of Luminar, the glint in the eye that always meant trouble was coming, the careless tilt of the head that said he knew he’d win before the game even started. Every inch of him radiated heat and ego.

Redd’s breath came sharp, but he didn’t move. The silver light from his spell flickered against the creature’s molten skin, reflected back like mock applause.

“Tell me,” Molten Redd continued, the smirk widening, “does it feel good? Pretending you’ve changed? Pretending this isn’t exactly who you’ve always been—self-righteous, reckless, hungry to be loved?” His tone dripped with familiarity, intimate as a confession. “You’re still chasing approval, Redd. You just swapped the audience.”

He leaned closer, ember breath curling against Redd’s cheek, voice dropping to a hiss. “You burned everything you touched long before the fire came.”

The heat between them spiked, blinding white. Behind the apparition, the flames rippled—flickers of memory beginning to push through the blaze, half-formed scenes clawing their way to the surface.
