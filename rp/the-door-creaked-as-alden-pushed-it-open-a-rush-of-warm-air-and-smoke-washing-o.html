Alden

The door creaked as Alden pushed it open, a rush of warm air and smoke washing over him. Laughter still lingered in the rafters, though the crowd had thinned; empty mugs abandoned where celebration had burned hottest. He stepped inside alone.

He crossed the room and lowered himself beside the central fire, the flames reflecting faintly in his eyes. The crackle of burning logs should have been comforting. Should have been.

Instead, his thoughts were still outside, caught beneath the stars and that impossible glow.

Golden wings. Not quite feathered, not quite radiant, but refined like sunlight sharpened into a blade. An empty, blank face, impossible to trust... And the words...

"It is your destiny to fall. We have already seen fit."

Alden exhaled slowly, fingers curling around the symbol of Lathander at his chest. “Lies,” he murmured under his breath, though the word felt thin in the open air. The Morninglord taught that every dawn was a promise - that no night, however cruel, could last forever.

Still… divine things did not usually waste breath on empty threats.

He stared into the fire, watching embers rise like dying stars.

"We passed..."
he reminded himself. The Winged Concord did not hand out victories. He had earned his place. His squad had earned theirs; through blood, bruises, and long nights in the saddle, trusting their wyverns with their lives.

His jaw tightened.

“If doom is coming,” Alden whispered to the flames, “then it will find us facing the dawn, not hiding from it.”

The fire popped, sending a brief flare of light upward. For just a heartbeat, the glow reminded him of that golden figure - and then it was gone, reduced to ash and warmth once more.

Alden sat straighter, resolve settling into his bones. Whatever that creature was... angel, herald, or something wearing borrowed light... it did not speak for Lathander.

Alden whispered to himself,

"You do not determine my destiny..."

"But you may live long enough for it to defy you."

Tomorrow, the sun would rise.
And Alden Ashdawn would rise with it.

Alden continues to stare into the fire, clutching his mug, although with a much more hopeful look on his face.

Talys moved about the party with precision as if each step was calculated and planned. As he travelled he chatted lightly with those he passed.

With a cocky grin he flipped the last silver piece high into the air with ease before catching it and rolling it across his knuckles. A practiced maneuver that he’d done hundreds of times around his fellow Talons, now Riders.

Talys then adjusted the sleeves of his silk shirt. He grabbed another drink before passing by Alden at the fire. He paused a couple of steps having heard the man whispering to himself of destiny and doom.

He swung around to face Alden with a puzzled expression.

“What are you talking to yourself about? Destiny or something?”

“No such thing as destiny. It’s a load of garbage made up to trick folks into thinking they gotta be happy with the hand they’re dealt. Destiny, the gods, whatever it is I don’t need ‘em. Never done a thing for me and look where I am now.”

He held his hands out wide indicating the celebration around. His casual dismissal of the gods would be nothing new to Alden. In the last two years Talys would’ve made very clear he was not a man of faith in anything much other than himself and the coin in his pocket.

Aurelio

From the shadows near the ceiling, Aurelio watched the events below. He had slipped away from the noise of the party some time ago, climbing into the rafters with quiet, practiced ease.

Up here, above the clatter of mugs and overlapping voices, the air felt more open; closer to the open sky he’d grown up beneath, closer to home.

Aurelio sat along one of the thick wooden beams, one knee drawn up, the other leg dangling loosely over open space. His fingers toyed with the thick blue ribbon wrapped around his wrist, the party's prize for slaying the manticore.

His jaw tightened. The image of the manticore still lingered vividly in his mind; the way it was pushed into its own attack by Talys, diving with Orion to claim the ribbon, the harsh, crushing sound he heard as the creature collided with the waters below.

He knew manticore’s weren’t native to the region, which meant it had been dragged from its home, shipped across borders, and set loose into the open sky, but not as a living creature, but as a spectacle for sport and target practice.

It wasn’t evil, he thought. It was just afraid. He knew from many years as a scout what fear looked like when something felt cornered.

Aurelio exhaled deeply as his gaze drifted toward the roofline; it was as though he were trying to see through it and into the open air. His people were taught from a young age that no creature was meant to be owned, and no life was meant to be caged. Freedom was not something granted or earned; it was something inherent.

The Winged Concord offered honor, purpose, and protection, but recently, he couldn’t shake the question gripping in his chest: If I must abandon my morals to stand here, then what am I really becoming?

Below, familiar voices cut through his thoughts; first Alden, then Talys. Aurelio leaned forward to listen in. He caught fragments of the conversation at first: destiny, garbage, gods never did a thing for me.

The full weight of the words settled into Aurelio, and his lips pressed into a thin line. He didn’t agree with Talys about everything, but he did on this.

Slowly, Aurelio slid off the beam and dropped down, boots touching down softly near the edge of the fire. At Aurelio’s heels, Castor stirred, and his ears twitched as he lifted his head from where he’d been curled near a support post. The blink dog gave a soft, sleepy huff, stretched his front legs, then sat at Aurelio’s side, tail thumping against the floor.

Aurelio’s gaze flicked between Alden and Talys, then he raised his hands, fingers moving expressively. As he signed in smooth motions, Castor tilted his head, and a faint glow of blue magic briefly surrounded his body.

Then, in a warm, playful voice, the dog spoke:

“He says he agrees with both of you.”

Castor glanced up at Aurelio, then back to the two men.

*“He says destiny isn’t something written for you, it’s what you build with the freedom you’re born with.”

Aurelio’s hands moved again, slower this time, and Castor translated.

“He said no one, not gods, not spirits, not angels, not monsters, gets to decide who you become.”

Aurelio’s eyes drifted briefly to Alden, and Castor continued.

*“He says he understands believing in something bigger than yourself, and that believing in light can give people strength.”

Aurelio signed once more, this time with a small, sharp motion at the end. Castor’s tail gave a soft wag.

“But where he’s from, they don’t follow gods.”

Castor finished the thought.

“They follow the winds because the wind can’t be owned, and it doesn’t obey crowns or commands. It moves where it chooses.”

Aurelio lowered his hands, and a calm expression spread across his features. Castor looked between the three of them and spoke again.

“So he's saying he respects your faith and thought, Alden, but he also understands Talys’.”

I will take what I want from this life and when I finally die it will be on my own terms having accomplished everything I possible could.”

While he wasn’t afraid to challenge their beliefs he wouldn’t lash out over hard words aimed at him either.

“Our lives have been very different paths, I don’t seek to change yours nor my own. If you intend to chart your course by wisdom and mine by strength then our squad will only be the stronger for it.”

Talys relaxed and waited to see what effect his words would have on his allies. While he wouldn’t mind their friendship it wasn’t a requirement. It was better to know now where they stood than when on a blood soaked battlefield.
