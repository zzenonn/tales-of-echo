**To Matilda, 
Majordomo of the Ravenshire Household**

Matty,

I’m not sure what made me finally pick up a quill to write you. Maybe it was seeing my dorm mates—Vornakir and Sunni—sending letters home last week. They looked so at ease with it, like reaching out somehow lessened their burden. It made me realize how long I’ve avoided doing exactly that.

Once I started thinking… well. I realized I really hurt someone this time. Badly. Not in the small, petty ways I used to, but in a way that feels like it cut deeper than I meant—or maybe deeper than I was willing to admit I could. Sitting with that has been miserable. It's also made me realize, I haven’t been the best son or brother, Matty.

To Father. To Blue. To you.
And pretending otherwise doesn’t make it any less true.

I don’t know how to fix any of it yet, but you’ve always had a way of grounding me, even when I was being impossible. So here I am, finally writing back to the woman who practically raised me, hoping you’ll still have some of that wisdom tucked away.

Life at Spark-Oath is… something. Strange and loud and chaotic, but not in a bad way. I should tell you who I live with!

**Vornakir** is a golden-scaled sorcerer who looks like he stepped out of one of those fancy Luminar portraits Father keeps in the hall. He’s talented—annoyingly so—but he’s also genuine in ways I didn’t expect. And Matty, he can cook. Really cook. Not as good as you of course, but he helped me bake fruit tarts once—don’t laugh, it was for a date—and they turned out perfect. He’s the kind of person who makes excellence look easy, and instead of making me feel small, it makes me want to do better. It’s strange.

**Caspian** is the steady one. The kind of person who listens first, reacts second, and somehow always knows the right thing to say. What you wouldn’t expect—what I didn’t—is that he learned that steadiness alone. His parents were… Matty, they were awful to him. Both of them. He never had anyone to fall back on, so he became that person for everyone else. Including me. It’s comforting in a way I didn’t know I needed.

And then there’s **Sunni**. Bright, warm, endlessly hopeful. He reminds me of a less ~~assholic~~ ~~dick~~ bad version of myself I never let grow because I kept guarding every bruise before anyone could see it. He’s like a little brother to me now.

But Matty… his father. The way that man speaks to him—it makes my stomach twist just hearing about it. Controlling, cruel, always reaching for the exact words that hurt the most. And somehow Sunni still finds ways to shine.

Seeing the two of them—what they survived, what they still carry—it’s made me realize something I never expected:
Father was trying. Awkwardly. Loudly. Sometimes the wrong way entirely. But trying. He was never cruel. Never cutting for the sake of it. Never the kind of man who twisted a knife just to watch someone flinch.

It made me realize..., I've had it good. Better than I ever admitted. I feel lucky to have dad in a way I never thought I would.


And then there’s **Zurr’ka**.
Someone important. Someone I hurt.
The details don’t matter—you’d see through them anyway. I let my fears twist everything, the same way they always do, and now there’s a distance I put there myself. I want to fix it, but I don’t know how yet. I’m trying not to ruin the good things in my life, Matty. Truly.

Anyway, enough about my life. 

How are things at home? How’s Father? And Blue?
Does he still go down to the river at sunrise? Does he still hum that terrible tune while he’s doing it? I miss it more than I ever let myself admit. I miss all of it. Even the things I used to roll my eyes at.

Write when you can.
And… thank you, Matty. For being the one person I never had to pretend with.

—Redd

Letter from Matilda “Matty”

**To Redmond Ravenshire**

Redd, my boy,

Well now… look at you, finally writing me a letter. Took you long enough. I nearly dropped the kettle when I saw your handwriting on that envelope. Don’t think for a second I wasn’t pleased, but stars above, you do know how to keep a woman waiting.

I could tell from the first few lines that something was weighing on you. You always did write different when your heart was bruised. But before we get to that, let me say something else first.

You may not believe this, but I remember every inch of who you were after your mother died — prickly, stubborn, hurt right down to the bone. You weren’t a bad boy, love. You were a grieving one. You pushed and lashed out because you didn’t know what else to do. And reading your letter now… I see a young man who’s finally turning around to face himself. I’m proud of you for that. Truly.

Now, about these friends of yours.

This **Vornakir** sounds like a polished one. I can picture you scowling at how perfect he stands. And you—cooking for a date? Preparing for a date? I nearly laughed out loud. I can't imagine you being the one nervous about *a boy* for once. 

Glad you remember, no one cooks better than me, of course, but I wouldn’t mind tasting something from a young man who can give me a run for my money. That’s rare.

As for **Caspian**, he sounds like someone who learned to be gentle without anyone teaching him how. Poor boy. It takes strength to grow kindness in a house that didn’t offer any. If he’s steady for you, Redd, then be steady for him too. Friendship isn’t a one-way chore.

And **Sunni**… oh, Redd. The way you wrote about him — like a little brother you didn’t know you wanted. Blue is going to be very jealous. That softness in you is your mother’s, you know. I’m glad it’s coming out. Hearing about his father made my blood boil. No boy deserves that. And you’re right: your father was never that kind of man. Loud, stubborn, impossible some days — yes. Cruel? Never. He loved you boys the only way he knew how, and maybe it wasn’t always enough, but it was honest. It’s good you can see the difference now.

Now, on to Blue. Since you asked: he was furious when your father sent you off. Wouldn’t speak a word to the man for a week. He pretends not to care now, but he asks after you in the little ways — if I’ve heard from you, how the Academy is, how you’re eating. He’d appreciate a letter. If you want, you can send it through me. Servants’ mail doesn’t get checked, and I know how to get things where they need to be.

And now, my love, we reach the part of your letter that made my heart twist.

You say you hurt someone badly. I can tell you mean it this time. I’m surprised to see you so honest about it — surprised in a good way. I won’t pry for details. If you wanted me to know them, you would’ve written them. But listen to me closely:

Talk to him.
Face this.
No running, no hiding behind that big wounded pride of yours.

Whatever you broke, the only chance you have at mending it is by standing in front of him and speaking plainly. Not perfectly — just truthfully. Consequences feel frightening, but they’re lighter when carried instead of avoided.

Your mother… gods, she would’ve been proud of you writing this letter at all. Proud that you can look at yourself without flinching quite so hard. You’ve got more of her kindness in you than you think.

Write back soon, and don’t make me wait another season for it. If you disappear on me again, I’ll march up to that Academy myself and drag you out of whatever trouble you’ve made.

Always,
Matty

Redd pushed open the door to his room and stopped short.

There was a box on his desk.

Not a parcel, not a wrapped bundle—a box. Dark wood reinforced with thin brass filigree, sealed with a Ravenshire crest he hadn’t seen in months. It sat there with quiet authority, as if it had every right to be in his space.

He closed the door behind him slowly.

The latch clicked. The box did not move.

Cautiously, he approached and lifted the lid.

Inside rested a crystal device, nestled in velvet the color of old wine. It was heavy when he picked it up, cut in flawless facets that caught the lamplight and scattered it into slow, deliberate rainbows across the walls. This wasn’t a common scrying stone or some academy-issued trinket. This was new magical technology—the kind still being whispered about in salons and council halls. The kind the Ravenshire family absolutely would have access to.

The runes along its base warmed beneath his fingers.
Before he could decide whether to sit, stand, or brace himself, the crystal flared.

Redd nearly dropped the thing when it activated

The image snapped into existence—and immediately tilted sideways.

For several heartbeats, the crystal showed nothing but a close, deeply unflattering view of Matty’s ear and a blur of kitchen rafters.

“Oh—no, no, that’s not right,” Matty muttered, her voice too loud and echoing. The image wobbled violently. “Lord Ravenshire, please don’t touch it. I said *don’t*—”

The crystal lurched. Ceiling. Then a flash of light. Then Matty’s face filled the frame far too closely, one eye narrowed in suspicion.

“…Is it blinking?” she asked the crystal. “The man *said* it would blink.”

She leaned back. The image steadied—mostly—framed a little too low, catching her from just under the chin.

“There. That’s better,” she said firmly, nodding to herself. “If you can see me, don’t say anything. If you can’t—well. This cost more than repairing the pantry roof, so it had *better* be working.”

She smoothed her apron, took a breath, and the moment she spoke his name, all the fuss melted away.

“Redd.” Her voice softened immediately. “Gods above, my boy. We heard about what happened at Spark-Oath. The Quasi. The Courts. All those banners everywhere.” She shook her head. “I’ve been worried sick. And before you say anything—no, I’m not the only one.”

She leaned closer, lowering her voice conspiratorially. “Half the household’s been hovering like hens, and the other half keeps pretending they’re not listening from the hall.”

A pause. Then, without turning her head, she added, “Lord Ravenshire. Harold! you might as well come into view.”

There was a stiff shuffle off-camera.

Harold Ravenshire stepped into frame—only halfway, as if unsure how much of himself was meant to be seen. He stood far too straight, hands clasped behind his back, expression carefully neutral.

“Redd,” he said, after clearing his throat. “You look… well. Given the circumstances.”

Matty shot him a look sharp enough to cut stone. “That’s not how you start.”

Harold stiffened, then visibly forced himself to relax. Just a little. “I meant—” He exhaled. “I’ve heard good things. About how you’ve been conducting yourself. About the people you keep company with.” A pause. “I’m… glad. Truly.”

Matty reached over and rapped him lightly on the arm. “Less like you’re addressing a council chamber.”

“I am trying,” he muttered, then looked back at the crystal. “I am glad you’re well.”

He didn’t get to say anything else.

“IS THAT HIM?”

The crystal jolted violently as Bluemond Ravenshire burst into frame, colliding squarely with their father’s side.

“Blue!” Matty barked. “Mind the crystal!”

“I SEE HIM,” Blue announced anyway, face suddenly filling the image as he leaned straight into the crystal, grinning wide and breathless. “Redd! You’re real! I knew this thing worked—”

He paused.

“…Why isn’t he answering.”

Harold groaned somewhere off to the side. “Because it’s not—”

Blue straightened abruptly. “Wait. Wait, what do you mean he’s not—” He squinted at the crystal, waved a hand in front of it. “Redd? Hello? Say something. Blink if you can hear me.”

Nothing happened.

Blue’s grin collapsed into a scowl. “Oh. You’ve got to be kidding me.”

“It’s a recording,” Matty said briskly, reaching up to gently shove Blue back by the shoulder. “We're talking into it now. He’ll see this when it activates on his end.”

Blue stared at the crystal like it had personally betrayed him. “So he can see *us*, but we can’t see *him* seeing us.”

“Yes.”

“And I can’t interrupt him.”

“No.”

Blue groaned loudly and dragged a hand down his face. “That’s cruel. That’s actually cruel. Who designed this?”

“Very clever people who charge far too much,” Matty replied. “Now behave. You wanted him to see you, and he will.”

Blue sighed, squared his shoulders, then leaned back into frame anyway, pointing at the crystal with renewed determination.

“Fine. Redd, if you’re watching this—this is me being calm.” He took a breath, failed to hold it. “Okay. First: are you sleeping? Because you look like you might not be sleeping. Second: are you eating? Properly. Not just bread and bad decisions.”

He leaned closer, squinting at the image like he could extract answers by force. “Third: you said you have friends now. Plural. How many is that? Two? Three? Are they nice? Do they know you can't cook or did you fix that part?”

A beat.

“…Are any of them replacing me?”

Harold muttered, “That’s not how—”

“Because if you’ve found a new brother,” Blue continued, ignoring him, “I just want to know where I stand. Am I still the favorite? Do they know your embarrassing stories? Because *I* know all of them. Like when I caught yoh staring at the baker boy before you hit your head on the—”

Matty cleared her throat sharply.

Blue winced, then added more quickly, “Not that I’m jealous. I’m just… checking. For information. Very normal information.”

He pointed at the crystal again. “You better write back and tell me about them. Names. Descriptions. Vibes. Especially if any of them cook better than me.”

Harold snorted. “You don’t cook.”

“Exactly,” Blue shot back. “So the bar is low.”

His grin softened then, just slightly. “I’m glad you’re okay. Don’t scare us like that again, alright?”

The image finally settled: Matty exasperated but smiling, Harold adjusting his sleeve and attempting to reclaim dignity, and Blue still vibrating with affectionate concern.

Matty leaned closer to the crystal, her voice warm.

“We just wanted you to see us,” she said gently. “To know we’re here. And to remind you—no matter how frightening things get there—you are not facing them alone.”

She squinted at the lens. “And next time, you write first after there's a school-wide catastrophe. This crystal cost a fortune.”

The light dimmed as the recording drew to a close, the crystal humming softly as it held the moment.
