# *Entry I: Joy*

I think it’s fitting that my first entry into this journal is Joy. If I’m going to document the color spectrum of emotions, I want the series to start with the feeling I like most!

Visually, joy almost always presents as warm, golden yellow with radiating rays of light; it’s like a sunrise breaking through a cloudy sky. Sometimes the rays are long and steady, sometimes they’re short and flicker like candlelight, but they always extend beyond the body, refusing to be contained.

I’ve noticed that when someone is joyful, their aura brightens from the center of their chest first, then it spills outward, washing over their shoulders and crown in shimmering arcs. Even the air around them seems lighter, and shadows loosen their grip.

Sometimes when I feel joy through my magic, it’s really strong and feels like sitting in an open field on a perfectly sunny day: warm grass beneath my palms, a gentle breeze carrying the scent of flowers. Sometimes it feels more mundane, like I just took a bite of my favorite dessert and that first taste of sweetness is spreading across my tongue.

I’ve noticed something else about joy, too: it’s contagious in a way few other emotions are. When someone’s joyful aura shines brightly, it nudges the colors of the other auras around them, making them lighter. I’ve seen it soften tension and even dim grief.

My favorite moments of joy to witness are the small, soft golden pulses someone has when they have a quiet victory or a nice private moment no one else has noticed.

It’s easy for me to be near joy because it’s one of the few emotions I don’t have to shield my mind from. It feels like warmth soaking into every aspect of my being.

I hope that if anyone were to look at my aura, they would see this color often.

# *Entry II: Anger & Passion*

Some emotions are closely related on the emotional spectrum, and a good example is anger and passion. At first glance, they look almost identical: both are red, intense, and capable of quickly spreading through someone’s emotional field. When my abilities first started developing, I struggled to tell them apart, but now that I’ve had time to observe them, the differences feel clearer.

Anger appears as a deep crimson aura, starting at the feet and flaring upward like a fire catching dry brush. The aura moves in sharp, aggressive bursts, licking upward in uneven tongues of flame. It rarely stays contained once it reaches someone’s core and will continue to climb rapidly, wrapping around the torso and shoulders until the entire aura flickers with intense, dark red heat.

When I feel anger through my empathy, it’s so overwhelming. It feels like a roaring inferno spreading through my head. My thoughts scatter the moment rage touches me, as if the heat burns through any ability I have to focus. Everything starts to feel like it needs my immediate reaction, no matter the response it might bring. By observing this emotion, I’ve learned that anger doesn’t want to be patient; it just wants to be released.

Passion, however, burns a bit differently. Its aura is still red, but it’s brighter and more vibrant, with a yellow undertone beneath the surface. Instead of rising from the ground, passion starts at the heart; igniting like a freshly lit hearth in the chest and then spreading outward in controlled waves of heat.

When I sense passion, it’s intense, but it’s far more subdued and nuanced than anger. Instead of overwhelming my thoughts, it sharpens them, like stoking a controlled blaze. When someone is passionate, I can feel the drive and determination behind their actions, and the focus that pushes them to pursue their heart’s desire.

The longer I watch people and observe their auras, the more I understand the difference in anger and passion. Anger climbs and consumes; passion ignites and expands. While both are flames, one burns everything with the misfortune of crossing its path, while the other provides purpose and direction by lighting the way forward.

# *Entry III: Avarice*

Avarice is very different from desire. Everyone desires something: comfort, security, clothes, good food, beautiful objects, indulgences after a long week. Those feelings rarely disturb auras in an extreme way; however, avarice is different.

When I see it, the aura’s color is burnt orange, and unlike many emotions that bloom outward from the heart or head, this one begins at the hands. The color gathers around the fingers first, then it creeps up the arms and clings tightly along the body. It never expands very far, except for when someone consumed by avarice is standing next to someone with something they want. Then their aura will surge forward quickly, grasping with orange strands that stretch outward like fingers against other people’s auras, as if it were trying to snatch pieces of their color and pull them back.

The texture is the most unsettling part about it. Most emotions flow or flicker, but avarice is spiked and jagged, like a thorned shell has surrounded the person. It looks like the aura is guarding whatever it has stolen or accumulated and bracing for anyone who might try to take it.

When I feel this emotion, it leaves an unpleasant taste in my mouth. It feels like being a dragon curled around a pile of gold, every thought circling the same pile, counting it, protecting it, measuring it against everyone else’s treasure. It also feels like being a greedy merchant or businessperson, someone who’s constantly calculating how they can squeeze one more coin out of the people beneath them.

There’s always a sense of never having enough, and no matter how much the person gathers, their aura remains tight and restless, never softening; it just sharpens further, bracing against imagined threats.

Thankfully, I don’t sense this emotion very often, but when it brushes against my mind, it makes my skin crawl and leaves me feeling grimy, like a mimic has spat me out, and I can’t wash the saliva off.

Avarice is one of the most selfish emotions in the spectrum: it takes, guards, and measures worth in what can be held rather than what can be shared. I fear that the longer it stays in someone’s emotional field, the smaller their world will become.

# *Entry IV: Jealousy*

Jealousy is an emotion that sits somewhere between longing and resentment.

It presents as a deep emerald green, similar in hue to a basilisk's scales, as if it's trying to embody the very essence of venom. Similar to avarice, jealousy tends to cling close to the body, curling and winding through the air like a snake. Thin strands of green wrap around the torso and shoulders and tighten whenever a jealous person’s attention lands on whoever they envy. The longer the feeling lingers, the more the aura twists and coils inward, winding over itself over and over again like a serpent guarding territory it believes is threatened.

It’s rare, but sometimes I have seen those coils reach outward. When jealousy deepens, I can see thin, emerald-green tendrils stretch toward the auras of others. It looks like the aura itself is trying to snuff out or steal pieces of someone else’s aura, pulling the light back toward itself as if it could somehow fill the emptiness with the emotions it saps.

Occasionally, another color stirs along with the green. If jealousy begins tipping toward something darker, something more possessive, thin, orange lines cut through the green, flashing briefly between the coils like sparks in a furnace. When that happens, the emotion is no longer just envy; it begins to drift toward avarice, wanting to take and keep what belongs to someone else.

Feeling jealousy through my empathy is deeply uncomfortable. It doesn’t burn like anger, and it doesn’t tighten my stomach like fear; instead, a heavy aching sits in my chest, feeling like longing for something I will never have, and worse, resenting everyone else for having it.

That’s the truth about jealousy; underneath the serpent coils and defensiveness is a wounded kind of yearning. Most people don’t admit that part aloud, but their aura shows it clearly every time.

Jealousy distorts perspectives until admiration begins to feel like competition, whispering into your ears that another person’s success must mean something was stolen from you. It's an emotion that rarely stays small for long; if left alone to fester, it tightens its coils, grows defensive, and sharpens its edges. And if those orange streaks begin to appear, the emotion stops longing for what it lacks and starts trying to take it. That is when jealousy becomes truly dangerous.

# *Entry V: Sadness, Grief, & Despair*

I’ve noticed sadness and grief lurking around campus recently, and not because people want to talk about it, usually most don’t, but because of the way it settles around them. It shows in how people carry themselves, how they cast their magic, how their voices are quiet when they’re trying to sound fine. It’s a heaviness people try to pretend isn’t slipping through the cracks in their armor.

Sadness exists on a shifting spectrum of blue hues, deepening and evolving as a person carries what they hold. Of all the emotions I’ve observed, this one feels the most fluid.

A soft, light blue color means someone is feeling a small bit of sorrow, something that brushes against a person and lingers only briefly, like after receiving a bad grade or disappointing news. When it deepens into a shade of navy blue, true sadness appears; it’s heavier, more present, and something that settles into the aura, slowing it down. Grief shifts the aura to a greyish-blue, as though the light within it has dimmed, like a rainy sky. And then there is despair, a deep, dark blue like the bottom of the ocean.

The shapes of the aura matter just as much as the color; it gathers into cloud-like shapes around the person, layering over itself in different densities. Sorrow forms a barely visible haze; sadness builds into fuller clouds, drifting and settling around the body; grief becomes heavier and denser, billowing clouds churning slowly around them, like a pressure system is pressing down on all sides; despair doesn’t drift or billow, it pulls inward, condensing the aura into something tight and overwhelming, like all of the weight the person is carrying has nowhere left to go.

Sensing these emotions through my empathy feels like I’m sinking in water. A spark of sorrow feels like dipping my hand into a cool stream; it’s noticeable but still moving, just passing by. Sadness feels like standing in a slow-flowing river, with the current gently tugging at me, like it’s asking me to follow it downstream. Grief is a deep lake, still on the surface, but heavy underneath, where sound feels swallowed and it takes effort to stay afloat. Despair feels like being abandoned in the open ocean, endless in a way that makes it hard to tell which way is up. The weight of it is immense, and it feels like I could be pulled under if I’m not careful.

Parts of me want to lean into the sadness to take some of it off their shoulders, but I have to remind myself that grief isn’t something you can lift out of someone. It’s something they have to work through on their own time. If I take that away from them, I stop being helpful and end up drowning right alongside them.

So I try to find the middle ground: be close enough that they don’t feel abandoned, but far away enough that I don’t confuse their pain for my own.

I also try to think about what I can offer them: a moment to breathe, a small bit of comfort when things feel too sharp. Sometimes that’s a spell or sitting with them and letting them know I’m not going anywhere.

I used to think it wasn’t enough; that real help had to fix things completely, but I’m starting to understand that comfort isn’t about fixing everything. It’s easing the edges of pain and giving them something to hold onto while they figure out how to stand again.

Maybe that’s all I can really give them, and maybe that’s alright and matters more than I thought: providing some little bit of support, some bit of peace, some bit of closure, some small piece of beauty in the middle of something dark.

An unexpected gift, just when it’s needed most.

# *Entry VI: Apprehension, Fear, & Anxiety*

Fear; this is the emotion I see every day. It doesn’t always present itself in dramatic bursts or displays; most of the time, it quietly threads itself through the decision-making process. Students here at Spark-Oath carry this emotion more than they realize.

The emotion is violet in color, but like the other emotions in the spectrum, it exists in a wide array of hues. Apprehension is a pale lilac; it flickers at the edges of the aura, and never fully settles, like a child with a question waiting to be answered. Fear deepens into magenta, sharper and more immediate than apprehension; it pulses through the aura in quick bursts, like an alarm waiting to be turned off. Anxiety is the color of amethyst, rich and heavy, saturating the aura until it’s nearly impossible to separate it from anything else the person is feeling.

What stands out about this emotion is *where* the aura resides. Unlike others that bloom from the heart or head, this one anchors itself at the feet. It’s an emotion tied to the fight-or-flight response. When someone freezes, the violet condenses into a block of ice around their feet, locking them in place. When someone chooses flight, the aura shifts and forms wing-like shapes around their ankles, flickering restlessly, as if urging them to run before the threat can react. When someone prepares to fight, the aura builds into a shield-like brace along their legs, holding them steady for impact.

Feeling this emotion through my empathy is harder to ignore than most others. Apprehension feels like a slight prick at the back of my neck; a subtle awareness that something *might* be wrong, even if I can’t name it yet. Fear is like a loud voice shouting in my head, telling me something dangerous is around, and pushing me to choose. Anxiety is the heaviest of the three; it settles into my chest like a weight I can’t shift, making it hard to breathe. It feels like everything is closing in around me all at once, and like the world itself has turned against me, and I don’t know how to stop it.

That’s the difference I’ve come to understand about this emotional spectrum. Apprehension asks questions, fear demands answers, and anxiety convinces you there is no good one.

I see this color more often than I’d like, but I guess that makes sense. Fear sits at the root of so many choices because people care about what happens to them: success, failure, loss, and impressions. It isn’t always a bad thing; it’s something that keeps us safe, sharpens us, and pushes us to act. When it lingers too long and becomes heavier, it stops guiding us and begins to trap us.

I’m still learning how to stand near this emotion without letting it overwhelm me, but that is a harder battle than I expected.

# *Entry VII: Love*
I found a book in the library that discussed the philosophy of love and how it existed in a near-infinite range of expressions, each with its own name and meaning. It concentrated on seven types of love: Eros, the love driven by passion, attraction, and desire; Philia, the love found in friendship; Storge, the quiet, unconditional familial love; Agape, selfless, all-giving love; Ludus, the love of playful, lightheartedness; Philautia, loving oneself; and Mania, obsessive love. 

At first, it felt like someone was trying to define the unexplainable, but the more I thought about it, the more I realized there were truths to the categories, and that I’ve seen all of these before in the way auras shift and move, and how they reach, cling, and hold onto another. So I started paying closer attention to try to match what I read with what I felt, and to see how each form of love looked when it manifested.

From what I’ve observed, love always ignites in the heart first, then moves outward; sometimes gently, sometimes all at once, sometimes unendingly.

Eros is the easiest for me to spot. It appears in rose and crimson hues, like velvet catching candlelight. It erupts from the heart in petal-like flares, pulsing and expanding outward in waves that resemble blooming flowers. When I feel it, it heats my skin like I’m beside a fire, and I feel a magnetic pull that centers my attention on one person, blurring everything else away. It’s intoxicating and a little overwhelming, like eating too much of your favorite dessert.

I see Philia all the time when I’m with my friends. The aura is warm, peachy pink, and it moves in slow, overlapping ripples, like water disturbed just enough to shimmer but not enough to break. Sometimes I even see the ripples form thread-like connections between friends, which I’ve found to mean they’re very close. It feels like ease when it brushes against me, like I’m breathing in the air of a wide open field in the middle of spring. It has no urgency to it, just a trusting presence that sits quietly against you like a warm hug.

I saw Storge a lot during parents' weekend, and now that I think about it, I had an abundance of it as a kid. It appears in muted, pastel pinks and is softened with hints of cream, like sun-faded fabric. It forms layered, protective fields around the body, projecting outward and wrapping around the target of the emotion. It feels grounding, like a sturdy platform beneath me that won’t give way no matter how much pressure is applied. It feels older than the other forms of love, ancient even, like it’s been around since the beginning of time.

I don’t encounter Agape much; the world has become so selfish lately, so it’s the most striking when I see it. It manifests as pale pink, so light it becomes white, with light gold edges that shimmer brightly when the emotion is strong. The aura radiates outward in wide, open arcs, expanding far beyond the person’s body. Feeling it is difficult to describe; it’s like being wrapped in a warm coat that doesn’t belong to anyone in particular. It softens everything; even harsh emotions seem to dull in its presence.

Ludus might be my favorite way to show love; there’s just something special about the laughter that comes from it. Ludis dances around in bright, vivid pinks like bubblegum and coral. The aura moves in quick, flickering patterns, darting around like tricky pixies, and never staying still for long. It feels like a light tickling on my skin when it brushes against my empathy, and there’s buoyancy to it, like being lifted off the ground.

I love seeing the confidence that comes from Philautia. It’s dusty pink in color, with subtle silver shimmer. The aura flows smoothly around the body, like the love they feel for themselves, forming a protective cocoon. When I feel it, it’s like everything inside settles into the right place, and I can exist as I am without judgment.

Mania is the most unstable form of love; obsession can ravage the mind and make you think things that aren’t true after all. Sometimes it flickers erratically between shades, like it can’t decide what it is, but I’ve found it often appears as a grossly oversaturated pink that borders on neon. The aura clings tightly to the feeler and the object of their desires, stretching too far outward, then snapping back, overcorrecting, and tightening again. It feels overwhelming, intense, and suffocating. While there’s love there, it’s tangled with fear, insecurity, and the need to be wanted no matter the cost.

I think what surprises me most is how often these forms overlap. Love is rarely just one thing: a friendship can carry pieces of selflessness; passion can hold playfulness and fear at the same time; even self-love can falter into something vain and obsessive.

But regardless of the form, every version of love I’ve seen begins the same way. Blooming from the heart and reaching outwards, never standing still. Even in its calmest forms, it’s always moving, giving, building, and searching for the next target to hold.

# *Entry VIII: Hope*

Hope is a quieter emotion than I expected. It doesn’t erupt like passion or coil like jealousy; it starts like a small, fragile ember, until it grows into a strong flame.

It presents as a rich indigo, like the sky right after sunset, and the last light of day hasn’t fully disappeared. When it first appears, it’s a faint, soft glow tucked close to the chest; it’s easy to overlook if you don't pay close attention. But hope builds, and as it strengthens, the aura stretches upward and outward like a beacon. It forms a column of light that rises through the body and extends beyond it. The brighter it becomes, the more stable it looks, like a flame that refuses to be put out by strong wind.

The most striking part of it is when hope shines so brightly that it cuts through the violet hues of fear. The violet light flickers, recedes, and sometimes disappears entirely when the indigo is strongest. It’s like watching the oppressed reclaim their future from their oppressors.

When I feel hope through my empathy, it hits like a second wind; like I was exhausted a moment ago, and suddenly my chest feels lighter and my thoughts clearer. There’s a sense that moving forward is possible again, even if nothing around me has changed yet. It makes everything feel reachable, like no matter how many times someone gets knocked down, they’ll get back up again because something inside them is cheering them on and urging them to refuse to stay down.

I understand why people say hope is stronger than fear. While fear constricts and traps, hope expands and frees. Whenever the two meet, hope doesn’t need to overpower fear all at once; it just needs to outlast it.

My mom used to tell me: *“Only when it is dark enough can we see the stars.”* I didn't know what she meant when I was younger, but now I know she was talking about hope, and during this challenging time in my life, I’m going to live by her words and fill the sky with the light of a billion, brilliant stars.

# *Entry IX: Apathy*

I’ve only seen this state once, and I hope I don’t see it again.

Apathy is absence, and not in the sense that nothing was ever there, but that everything has been stripped away, leaving a hallowed husk.

It is black in color, the shade that absorbs all the light in the spectrum. When it appears, it doesn’t behave like other auras and glow, pulse, or move with any natural rhythm. It spreads slowly and quietly like a creeping shadow that begins at the edges of the aura and works its way inward, swallowing color as it goes. I’ve seen it once; vibrant hues dull, then dim, then disappear entirely as shadows overtook them. There are no sharp edges, spikes, or flares to it either. It just exists in a state of quiet consumption.

The worst part is how it feels, or rather how it doesn’t. Every other emotion I’ve recorded has weight, texture, movement, something I can compare it to when it brushes against my empathy, but this one? There’s nothing to sense: no pull, no pressure, no heat, no cold; just emptiness. The closest I can come to describing it is being alone in space, existing in a place where there is no sound, no direction, and no sense of anything around you.

You’d think it’d be a stage of sadness, but it’s not. Sadness hurts, weighs, and presses. Apathy doesn’t do any of that; it’s what’s left when even those feelings have burned out.

I didn’t know what to do when I first encountered apathy, and I still don’t. Every instinct I have to comfort someone relies on there being *something* to respond to, but with this, it’s like speaking into silence.

If I ever encounter it again, I know one thing for certain: I’ll have to move carefully, not force anything, and try not to fill the space all at once.

Maybe the key is creating the smallest flicker of hope that can exist alongside the emptiness; something that doesn’t demand a response and can wait until they’re ready for it.

I don’t know if that’s enough, but I think if even one emotion can return, no matter how faint, then maybe the rest can follow.

# *Entry X: Peace*

Peace is the balance of all emotions.

It presents as pearlescent white, its color shifting softly when it catches the light; hints of other colors shimmer beneath the surface: faint blues, soft pinks, traces of gold, showing how every emotion has settled into something harmonious rather than competing for space.

It’s the only aura I’ve seen that doesn’t feel like it’s trying to move towards or away from someone. It forms behind the person in the shape of a blooming lotus, with layered petals of white light delicately unfolding.

When I feel peace brushing past me, everything in me settles, like a ripple smoothing out across the water until there’s no waking left. My thoughts quiet, my breathing evens, and my constant awareness of other people, their emotions brushing at the edges of my mind, fades into the distance. There’s no urgency, or pressure, or expectation, just space to exist exactly as I am without needing to change anything.

What most people think peace means is that the person is free from all their worries, but that’s as far from the truth as you can get. I’ve seen it alongside grief, love, and even fear. But instead of those emotions overwhelming the person, peace seems to hold them in balance, with everything finding its place rather than fighting for it.

I think that’s what makes this one so rare: peace isn’t something that happens when everything is perfect; it’s what happens when someone learns to exist with imperfection.

If hope is the light that pushes us forward, then peace is what waits when we finally reach the destination, and for a moment, there’s nothing you need to chase.
