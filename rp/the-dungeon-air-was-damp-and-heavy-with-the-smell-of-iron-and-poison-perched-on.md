The dungeon air was damp and heavy with the smell of iron and poison. Perched on Redd’s broad, furred back, Sol leaned dangerously forward, bracer sparking as his hand stretched for Vornakir. Golden sigils burst from his wrist in frantic arcs, warmth flooding into torn flesh as cure wounds started to knit the skin back together.

The reprieve lasted only a heartbeat.

From the shadows, the assassin’s daggers struck true, steel slicing through cloth and skin, venom searing like fire in his veins. Sol gasped, vision fracturing into shards. He fought to hold on, to trace one more sigil to shield or heal his friends, but the poison tore the strength from him.

He collapsed forward against Redd’s back, bracer dimming as his fingers slipped from their desperate reach. A ragged whisper caught in his throat:

“I’m…sorry.”

Then the light guttered out. His weight sagged against the dire wolf’s fur, breath shallow, body still. Only the remnants of his magic lingered, faint motes drifting like fireflies over Vornakir’s shoulder. They flickered once, twice, then vanished into the dark as silence closed around him.

The dream began in silence.

Sol’s eyes opened to familiar but wrong halls. The Aelia estate stretched around him, grand in shape but hollow in substance. Marble floors were cracked, ivy crept through broken windows, and the chandeliers sagged with rust. The air smelled of dust and mildew, a house abandoned by time.

Then, laughter. High, unrestrained, achingly familiar.

His heart lurched as he turned toward it. Down the ruined corridor, a boy darted into view: wide-eyed, golden-haired, no older than six. His own younger self, barefoot and grinning, waving before darting away again.

“Come on!” the child called. “Hide and seek!”

The floor groaned under Sol’s boots as he followed, the walls crumbling further with every step. His younger self giggled, darting in and out of doorways, until finally he pushed one open and vanished inside. Sol followed, and the rot peeled away.

The room became the lakeside of his family’s estate. Twilight spread in warm purples and oranges, water lapping gently against the shore. His father, younger and unburdened, crouched by the fire pit, his mother leaning against his shoulder, a smile of laughter in her eyes. His brother Lucien stood at the water’s edge, teaching little Sol how to angle a flat stone. The boy’s hands were clumsy, his first throws splashing uselessly, until one skipped. Once. Twice. Three times.

Child Sol squealed, hopping in place, then he tore across the shore to his parents. His mother bent to press a kiss to his brow. “Good job, sweetheart,” she whispered, pride bright as the firelight. His father swept him up, spinning him through the air, voice booming with warmth Sol could scarcely remember anymore.

“Look at my talented kiddo!” Sirius had laughed, unguarded, uncalculating.

The fire popped. The water glowed. For a fleeting moment, it was real again.

Sol stood there, frozen, the ache in his chest almost unbearable. “We were… happy,” he murmured, voice catching on the words. “We really were.”

From the fire’s edge, his child self looked back at him, eyes too knowing for his age. “It’s one of my favorites,” the boy said softly, before grinning wide and darting off again. “But you still haven’t found me!”

The lakeside shimmered, cracking like glass, and the ruined estate bled back in as the child’s laughter echoed through the collapsing halls.

The child’s laughter echoed ahead of him, skipping down corridors that splintered and sagged, ivy curling up their edges like veins. Sol hurried after, boots scuffing across broken tile. The boy darted into another doorway, pushing it wide with both small hands before disappearing inside.

Sol followed, and the world reassembled.

He knew this room, his father’s study. The fire burned low in the hearth, casting more shadows than light. The shelves that once gleamed with polished wood seemed darker, heavier, each ledger and scroll weighing the air. Sirius sat at the desk, papers stacked high, hands held against his temples. His face was tight, cold in a way that hadn’t existed on the lakeside.

“What do you mean… foreclosure?” Sirius snapped at the steward before him, his voice sharp as glass. His jaw worked furiously, eyes distant in calculation, already searching for angles of survival and control. The warmth that once filled his voice was gone, stripped away by years of strain.

Next to him, little Sol sat cross-legged on the carpet, a sketchbook balanced on his knees. He hummed softly as he drew, pencil scratching out long, messy silhouettes of flowing coats, braids, and bold collars like the ones Sirius had once designed for his shops. The same boutiques and salons that were now shuttering one by one.

The steward’s voice was careful, almost pitying. “My lord… without steady business, without patrons, the closures will continue. The debts are already—”

“Silence,” Sirius snapped, his teeth bared. He gripped the edge of his desk so tightly the wood creaked. It was not simply money slipping through his hands, but the failure of every venture he had staked his family’s honor on. Sirius’s artistry, once their pride, had drawn too few clients to sustain them. To Sirius, it was proof that dreams of fabrics and lines, of hair and color, could never bear the weight of legacy.

Sol looked up at his father then, eyes bright with a child’s instinct to soothe. “Papa… I’ll make us money someday too. Look.” He turned his sketchbook, showing a messy outline of a gown with starbursts embroidered down its hem. “I’ll be a fashion designer, like you!” Child Sol crept forward and tugged gently at his father’s sleeve, small arms wrapping around him in a desperate hug.

The words landed like salt on a raw wound. Sirius’s gaze cut to the page and then away, cold and heavy. For a heartbeat, he did nothing. His hand twitched, as if to return the embrace. “Not now, Sunni,” he muttered, voice low and clipped.

When Sol crawled closer anyway, small arms wrapping around his father’s waist in an earnest hug, Sirius stiffened. The dam inside him cracked. “I said not now!” His voice thundered through the chamber, louder than he’d meant. His hand shoved down on the desk, rattling the inkpot. “Leave my office now, Sol’Régem!”

Sol’s arms loosened. His lip trembled. Then he turned and bolted, tears streaking as he clutched the sketchbook tight to his chest.

Sirius’s face fell the second he vanished. “Wait, Sunni, I didn’t mean—” His hand twitched forward, but the door slammed behind his son. He pressed his palms against his face, guilt lancing through the fury.

The doorway filled with another figure, his mother, Cassiopeia, her silhouette sharp in the firelight. She glanced after the boy who had darted past, then fixed her husband with a fury so rare it stole the air from the room.

“What did you just do?” she demanded, voice low and dangerous. “He’s six, Sirius. *Six*.”

Sirius’s lips parted, words faltering, but she cut him down with a glare that held no room for excuses.

Adult Sol lingered in the corner of the memory, fists tightening at his sides. He swallowed hard, throat thick, watching the fracture in his family open for the first time. “This was it,” he whispered to himself. “The start. The moment warmth became weight.”

The hallways groaned underfoot, warped beams splitting as though even memory itself could barely hold the weight. Sol chased the sound of sobs, turning corners, passing doors that yawned open into empty, ruined rooms. At last, the sound drew him to a smaller study near the kitchens, a place he remembered all too well.

He slowed, breath catching. The cupboard door at the far wall sat barely ajar. Tiny hiccupped breaths leaked through. Sol crouched, pulling it open.

There he was: the boy version of himself, curled tight with his knees to his chest, tear-streaked cheeks pressed against his arms. The child startled when the door opened, only to look up not at adult Sol, but at another figure kneeling beside him.

“Thought so,” Lucien said gently, voice already warm with the surety of someone who knew his brother better than anyone else. He was taller now, nearing sixteen, but his eyes carried a gravity beyond his years. He smiled faintly, brushing a lock of child Sol’s hair back. “I knew you’d be here. Always your favorite hiding place.”

Lucien squeezed into the narrow space, sitting shoulder to shoulder with his brother, even if it meant folding himself awkwardly. His arm curled around the smaller boy, pulling him close. “Hey. You’re alright. He didn’t mean it. He’s just…” Lucien’s jaw tightened briefly, then softened again. “…He doesn’t always know how to carry things without breaking.”

Child Sol sniffled, leaning into him.

Lucien glanced at the bracer, which glimmered faintly against his wrist. With a practiced motion, he traced his fingers into the air, and threads of bronze, silver, and gold spilled into the air. “Here. Let me show you something better.”

The walls of the cupboard dissolved, peeling away like mist. Cold winter air rushed in, crisp and bright, carrying the scent of pine and sugar. The estate hall bloomed around them, lit with amber light. Outside the windows, snow drifted slowly, softly, and perfectly.

At a long table in the middle of the room, their mother hummed while laying out trays of cookies, bowls of icing set like little treasure troves of color. Sirius was there, sleeves rolled to his elbows, carefully applying an icing pattern that looked suspiciously like a misshapen and warped penguin, only for Cassiopeia to laugh and fix it with a flourish of sprinkles. The sound of their laughter, the warmth of it, filled the space in a way that made it ache to remember.

Child Sol’s face lit up, eyes wide. “I remember this! Mama let me use the gold icing, even though I made a mess.”

“You got more on yourself than the cookies,” Lucien teased, tugging affectionately at his brother’s sleeve. “But you were so proud of them anyway.”

Adult Sol stood apart, watching the memory unspool with a hollow fullness in his chest. His throat worked as he whispered, “Lucien was always there. Every time. When I fell apart, when Father turned sharp, when I needed something solid to hold onto, he carried it. He carried *me*. I never knew how he shouldered so much and still managed a smile for me.”

The boy’s laughter rang out again, happy now, as he grabbed Lucien’s hand and pulled him toward the cookie table. The bracer pulsed faintly, threads of memory shimmering brighter around them, wrapping the brothers together in that fleeting warmth.

Adult Sol reached toward the scene, his fingers grazing the light as if to hold onto it, even as he felt the child-version slip away, dashing deeper into the collapsing estate once more.

The drawing room.

“Come here, Sunbeam.” He patted his knee.

Child Sol scrambled forward eagerly, clambering onto his lap with a sketchbook tucked under his arm. He flipped it open, and amateur coats, gowns, and little notes about fabric and trims sprawled across the pages.

“Look, Papa. I made these! I want to make clothes like you!”

Sirius blinked, startled, then let out a chuckle. His hand ruffled Sol’s hair, thumb brushing over his crown. “Always so full of ideas. You get that from me.”

The warmth lingered for a moment, fragile but real. Sol leaned closer, beaming. “Do you like them? Do you think they’re good enough?”

The smile faltered. Sirius’s gaze lingered on the sketchbook, then flicked to the ledger at his knee, the red-inked numbers, the debts that strangled their name. Lucien’s shadow fell across the doorway, now at seventeen. Sirius’s jaw set.

“Sunni,” he began slowly, too carefully. “Not you too.”

The boy’s grin wavered. “What do you mean?”

Sirius closed the sketchbook with deliberate gentleness, but his hand pressed heavily on the cover. “These dreams… design, salons, finery. They’ve no future. Look around, our boutiques shutter one by one, our name slips lower in status each month. Do you know what that means? Failure.” His voice grew harder, cutting. “And I will not see you destroyed by the same illusions that ruined me.”

The boy slipped from his lap, clutching his sketchbook like a shield. The tears spilled fast now, and before either man could stop him, he bolted for the door.

Adult Sol lingered in the doorway, the crackle of the fire hollow in his ears. His voice was a rasp, barely holding steady: “He tried. He tried to be kind, but it never lasted. Every time I thought I had him back… the cold always won.”

Ahead, the phantom child’s sobs echoed through the collapsing halls. Sol followed, dread rising sharp in his throat.
