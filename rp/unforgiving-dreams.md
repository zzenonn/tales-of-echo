### &#x270D; Alden
<sub>Feb 25, 2026 &middot; 05:15 AM UTC</sub>

The road back from Hillbergh had been silent.

Not the peaceful kind of silence that settles after a long patrol. This was different. Heavy. Pressed down by the memory of smoke and ruin.

Hillbergh was no longer a town. It was ash stitched to earth. Burned beams clawed at the sky. Doors torn from hinges. The smell of it - gods, the smell - still clung to Alden’s cloak no matter how hard the wind tore at him during the flight to Cindros.

The Saintillians had not merely conquered. They had erased.

By the time the squad returned to their quarters in Cindros, night had already swallowed the city. Armor was set aside. Weapons leaned carefully within reach. They spoke with their instructor before bed.

Was the city ready?
Was the Council evacuating fast enough?
Would the walls hold?

Alden had stood near the center of the room when he answered.

“We cannot lose hope,” he had said, steady even when his chest felt tight. “If we falter before they even arrive, then we’ve already surrendered. We are the shield that guards this realm. That’s what the Concord is meant to be.”

He believed it.
He had to.
But belief did not make the images fade.

------------------

When Alden finally lay down, exhaustion dragged him under quickly... And the dream began just as quickly.

He was back in Hillbergh - But the sky was wrong.

It burned a violent red, not dawnlight gold. The smoke moved like living things. He could hear shouting - no, screaming. He ran. Boots pounding against cracked stone.

He reached a doorway just as it collapsed inward.
Inside was someone he knew.
Not a faceless villager.
Not a stranger.

She turned toward him through the smoke - someone from his past. Someone he had once sworn to protect. The same eyes. The same outstretched hand.

Elena.

And just like before... He was too late.
The floor gave way - flames swallowed the space between them.

“Alden-!”

He lurched forward, and awoke with a violent gasp.

"Elena!" Alden shouted, face full of fear - his heart and mind filled with failure once again, sweat dripping from his brow, his pillow soaked.

The room is dark. Cold. Real.

His heart slams against his ribs like it’s trying to escape. His hands are shaking. For a brief, disorienting moment he doesn’t know where he is - Hillbergh? The past? Cindros?

His breath won’t slow.
Failure.
The word pounds through him louder than his pulse.

Failure when he was younger. Failure that cost a life he cared about. A moment he still replays, wondering what he could have done differently. Faster. Braver. Smarter.

And now Hillbergh.
And soon, Cindros.
What if he’s too late again?

Alden pushes himself upright, swinging his legs over the side of the bed. His hands press hard against his knees as he forces air into his lungs.

“Inhale,” he mutters to himself. “Hold. Exhale.”

It doesn’t help, so he does the only thing that ever truly steadies him.

He drops to his knees beside the bed. The floor is cool beneath his palms.


<sub>Feb 25, 2026 &middot; 05:16 AM UTC</sub>

“Lathander,” he whispers, voice raw. “Morninglord… I am afraid.”

The honesty leaves him trembling.

“I cannot fail them. Not again. Not like before.” His jaw tightens. “If I must carry the weight, then strengthen my back. If I must stand between darkness and the innocent, then make me unbreakable.”

His breathing begins to slow.

“I know you do not promise that no one will fall,” he continues, quieter now. “But let me be there. Let me not turn away. And if I cannot save them all… grant me the courage and the power to rise the next day and try again.”

He bows his head until his forehead nearly touches the floor.

In the silence that follows, his heartbeat begins to steady.

Outside, somewhere in the distant dark, the faintest hint of dawn wind brushes against the shutters.

Not sunrise yet.
But the promise of it.

Alden rises slowly. The room feels less suffocating now, though sleep will not return easily.
He pulls on a simple tunic and steps into the quiet corridor. The city is asleep. The quarters are still.

In the kitchen, he pours himself a cup of water. The sound is almost too loud in the silence. He drinks slowly, staring at the faint reflection of starlight in the surface.

His hands have stopped shaking.
But his eyes remain distant.

He sets the cup down, bracing both palms against the counter, breathing deep.

The shield.
That’s what he told everyone when they had first arrived to Cindros.
A shield does not get to break.

Footsteps echo faintly somewhere down the hall - soft, uncertain. Perhaps someone else awake. Perhaps someone drawn by the same unrest.

Alden straightens, wiping a hand across his face, composure settling back into place like armor. 

Alden thinks to himself, "I must have faith, remain hopeful. I must not show weakness, not now - not when so many are depending on us."

However, his eyes are still distant, as if they're somewhere else - perhaps still trapped in the past.

### &#x270D; Calarel
<sub>Feb 26, 2026 &middot; 01:28 AM UTC</sub>

Sleep hadn’t come easily for Calarel either. 

Every time she closed her eyes, she saw flames where homes should have been. Heard silence where there should have been people, save for one name she heard countless times in her dreams and in the back of her thoughts. 

*Callie!* 

A name she hadn’t been called in a long time, If they should encounter any Saintillians on their next mission…perhaps one of them would know something. Anything.

Her jaw tightened. 

Barefoot and restless, she moved through the corridor in quiet, measured steps. Her sleeping clothes were simple– dark linen fitted close enough not to hinder movement, even at this hour. Her dark blue hair had been pulled into a loose braid that now rested over one shoulder, slightly disheveled from a night that had offered no rest. 

She heard Alden before she saw him. Water pouring, and a breath held too carefully. 

Calarel slowed just outside the kitchen entrance, lavender eyes narrowing slightly as she took in the shape of him through the dim light. She stepped into the doorway without ceremony. Alden looked put together at first glance, and anyone else might have left it there. 

Calarel wasn’t anyone else. 

Her gaze cataloged the signs: the tightness in his shoulders, the distance in his eyes, and the way his hands still rested too firmly against the counter. He wasn’t panicking, but the echo of it was still in the room. 

She leaned one shoulder lightly against the doorframe, arms loose at her sides. Her eyes flicked briefly to the cup, then back to his face. “The night didn’t sit well with you either?”

"I've seen a lot of death - I've travelled battlefield from battlefield treating wounded, I've helped treat whole towns struck with disease, you name it - But it never gets easier; seeing death." Alden tenses suddenly, and he turns his body behind him as if he heard something and his gaze narrows for a moment, as if fixated on something - clearly part of his mind is still trapped in that dream. After a brief moment, he relaxes and rubs his ears, feeling as if he heard a familiar voice.

He fixes his gaze back on Calarel, "How about you? What's got you up at this ungodly hour?"

<sub>Feb 26, 2026 &middot; 01:48 PM UTC</sub>

Calarel watched the moment Alden’s focus slipped. It was quick, and most people would have missed it. The turn, the narrowing of his gaze, and the way his body went tight like he was bracing for something that wasn’t there. 

*Not just bad sleep,* she noted. *Haunted.* 

She didn’t call him out on it. Not yet.

Instead, she pushed off the doorframe and stepped fully into the kitchen. The dim light caught the pale blue of her skin and the loose braid draped over her shoulder, slightly frayed from a night that had offered neither of them much rest. 

“At this hour?” she said dryly as she stopped a few paces away, reaching for a cup and filling it with water. “Everything’s ungodly.”

The quiet pour sounded too loud in the sleeping quarters. Her gaze slid briefly to the window, where the night still pressed heavy against the glass. 

“I…couldn’t shut off my thoughts,” she admitted simply. Her arms folded loosely, but her fingers flexed once against her sleeve. “What about you?” she asked, lifting her eyes back to him. “What has you haunting the halls during hours even the gods avoid?”


### &#x270D; Alden
<sub>Mar 03, 2026 &middot; 02:14 AM UTC</sub>

Alden lets out a quiet breath at her question, the faintest ghost of a smile tugging at his mouth before it fades.

“Even the gods avoid,” he repeats softly. “Truer words have never been said.”

His gaze drops to the surface of the water in his cup, watching it settle.

“My dream wasn't just about Hillbergh alone,” he admits after a moment. “It just… opened a door.” His jaw tightens slightly. “There was someone I once failed. A long time ago. A good friend of mine - one I miss more than anything.” He says carefully, as if he could break. “I told myself I’d made peace with it. That I understood I couldn’t save everyone.”

He shakes his head once, subtle.

“But in the dream, I was there again. Reaching. Too slow. Too late.” His fingers flex unconsciously against the counter. “Hillbergh felt the same. Like standing in the smoke - lungs full, hands empty.”

He looks back at Calarel then, steady but unguarded.

“I think what struck me differently today,” he says quietly, “is the fear that it’s about to happen again. It's a feeling I just can't seem to shake. Like you, I just couldn't shut my thoughts off - speaking of which, if you don't mind my asking - What has your mind racing this god awful night?”


### &#x270D; Calarel

When Alden finished, Calarel didn’t fill the silence out of politeness. She let it linger because some things deserve to. The silence of the night pressed in around them, but she did not move or look away.

“Standing in smoke with empty hands,” she repeated, almost to herself. “Yes. I know that feeling.” 

Her thumb dragged once along the cup’s edge before she set it down. The ceramic clicked faintly against the table.

“I am sorry about your friend. What was their name? You don’t have to say it if it still cuts.”

When he turned the question back on her, her expression sharpened. 

“The Saintillians.” The word sat in her mouth like something bitter she refused to spit out. “They took someone from me—  my sister.”

Her jaw tightened, and then her lavender eyes lifted to his. There was no shine of tears there. Only unflinching resolve. 

“I don’t intend on being too late twice.”


### &#x270D; Alden
<sub>Mar 10, 2026 &middot; 04:10 AM UTC</sub>

"Being too late... A sensation I'm all too familiar with. I believe you when you say it - the resolve you possess is strong, I can see that much."

Alden takes a pause, sets his glass down on the counter and leans up against it. He gazes down towards his hands - empty, unsure of what to do with them - he clasps his hands in front of him.

"Her name was Elena, she was a good friend of mine - A great friend." His eyes begin to swell with memories and a hint of sadness. "We grew up together in Oldview, her and I. Our families were close but then again as was everyone else in the community. However her and I had a connection that we didn't share with anyone else." He takes a pause, a deep breath. 

"When we were young, we did everything together; we played, we worked, we laughed, we broke bread together - we lived our lives together. Wherever she was, I was there, and wherever I was, she too was there. We thoroughly enjoyed praying together as well - I attribute my service to Lathander all to her." Alden lets out a slight chuckle, "Believe it or not, she was more religious than I am now. You can blame her for that." The smile that crept across his face after he laughed begins to fade away, sadness reappearing within his eyes.

"She was always helping other people. Whether they needed help with their gardens, attending livestock, or if the infirmary was looking for volunteers - she was there. Of course, with us being attached at the hip I was there too, but in all honesty I was her shadow. She was the bright star - rightfully so." Alden unclasps his hands from in front of him and sets them on the table behind him to support him as he leans against it.

"Years went on, and we never drifted apart. If anything, the bonds we shared became stronger. She became a fulltime cleric, working in the towns' hospital and local infirmaries. At the time, I was still just a farm hand, though I volunteered where I could. I helped her study and sometimes even offered to be her test subject when it came to testing spells she was learning." 

A brief smile creeps across his face and then Alden takes a deep breath and a long pause, as if preparing for something.

"Right about when her and I both turned twenty, a disease struck our town. A nasty, terrible illness began to infect many of those in Oldview - hundreds died, many more suffered. Elena worked endlessly in the infirmaries treating those who were sick - it was a miracle she wasn't infected... sooner." 

Alden takes another pause.

"Of course her luck eventually ran out. It only makes sense - considering the proximity she was in with sick individuals - it was only a matter of time." 

A painful look streaks across Alden's face.

"When I had found out she was sick, I too began to volunteer in the infirmaries - more so than I already was. I personally saw to her as well, ensuring that she was getting enough food and water. I hardly slept and had nearly zero free time - and in whatever free time I had, I spent it researching the illness spreading through our town. Something about it seemed unusual; it's sudden appearance, the lack of any viable treatment for it - It didn't seem natural." 

Alden moves away from the table, and walks towards a nearby window. He stares out into the dark landscape, as if looking for something - someone.

"That's when I discovered it. This wasn't a disease created by nature, but rather a plague conjured by man. It was magic, more specifically, a dangerous type of necromancy."


<sub>Mar 10, 2026 &middot; 04:17 AM UTC</sub>

"It took time to figure out who caused it, but after I told the authorities what I had discovered, they managed to narrow it down to one man - Brother Cauldrine. He was a fellow follower of Lathander, and a well respected individual in our community. Years before, he had lost his family in a terrible accident - he was never the same since the incident. The authorities believed he turned to necromancy, and perhaps some unknown God of death, in hopes of being able to sacrifice enough souls to bring his family back."

Another pause, and a look of betrayal comes and goes across Alden's face.

"I respected him, we all did. Whether or not he was successful, we don't know. He wasn't at his residence, but we found manuscripts and the writings of a crazed man within his home. Once we discovered that the source was magic in nature, treatment became easier."

"Eventually, we managed to stabilize Elena and most others who were sick at this point. However, our one mistake we made was not telling the townsfolk what we had discovered. The authorities, church and other officials wanted to keep it a secret, in hopes of preventing anymore chaos - the thought of someone so well respected in our community, being responsible for such actions would've surely caused an uproar..."

"And then on that one, fateful night - the townsfolk decided to take matters into their own hands. Over a thousand had died, and the town was tired. Tired of losing the ones they loved, and fearful of losing anymore - they banded together and determined their best chance of squashing the disease was to burn all of the infirmaries to the ground, killing those still infected in hopes of preventing the spread any further."

Alden turns away from the window and faces back towards Calarel. She can see his eyes - bloodshot and watery.

"I was at the market that night. We needed medical supplies and other herbs, and the night was slow so I volunteered to go out and purchase them myself. That was my second mistake."

"By the time I had arrived back to the infirmary that Elena was at, villagers were already there - pitchforks and torches in hand and the building on fire. They had hung some of the doctors, blaming them for the spread of the disease - fools, the lot of them."

"I managed to push through the crowds and get to the infirmary - Gods... it was hot. The fire was massive, the flames were roaring... I couldn't tell if I was hearing the screams of those within, or if it was the fire screaming at me - telling me not to go in, telling me that it was pointless. But nonetheless, I ventured forth." 

Alden winces, as if reliving the moment and the feeling of flames licking his skin.

"I managed to charge through a few doors and eventually I made my way to the sickbay where Elena was. I could see her, sitting up in her bed - eyes full of fear and confusion. She saw me and called out to me for help, but the building was coming down all around us."

"The support beams ended up coming down in front of me before I could get to her, and we were separated, but she was still visible. I could see the flames getting closer to her, the smoke getting thicker. Eventually the smoke became too thick and I could no longer see and I had to rely on touch - which was difficult, considering everything was burning."

Alden rolls up his sleeves and lifts up his shirt, revealing gruesome burns all across his body. Alden rarely takes off his armor, and up until this moment, he has never removed his under garments in front of the squad. This is why - his body is adorned with burn scars.

"I managed to get across the wreckage that had separated us and I finally got to her - but I was too late. She had died from the smoke and intense heat. I held her, there on the bed she died at - completely ignoring the fiery hell that surrounded me. At that point, I didn't feel any physical pain. The emotional pain I felt far outweighed the flames sinking its fangs deeper and deeper into my body."

"Eventually I came to my senses after what felt like an eternity, but surely was only a few seconds. I carried her out the same way I came in, protecting her body from the flames with my own."

Alden lowers his sleeves and shirt, and turns away from Calarel, almost as if he feels shame for the scars that adorn his body.

"Once I had made it outside, the authorities had quelled the riot and told the villagers what had happened. I'm sure they were shocked - sickened by the actions they had just commit."

"But I can't speak for certain on it, because in that moment, I was in agony - not just physically, but mentally. I had lost the one person I held closest to me. I had failed her, myself, and her family." 

"The following day they retrieved the dead from the wreckage and buried them. I dug Elena's grave myself, even with my injuries - I owed her that much."

"For the next few months, I was in shambles. Between the treatments I had to undergo to heal my burns, and the emotional toll I was suffering... I had no sense of direction in life, no sense of anything."

Alden takes another pause, however the tears that were building up in his eyes began to fade away, and a new emotion began to spark within them - hope.

"That's when I was approached, that fateful morning. I saw Lathander walking through the fields that Elena and I had both played in as children. I saw him touching the very crops her and I grew, as if wading through open waters. A sign, I told myself. A sign that despite the chaos and destruction I had witnessed and lived through, hope was always one step ahead. This is what drove me to truly pursue my duty as a cleric of Lathander - to honor not only him, but more importantly Elena and the lives lost on that cursed night."

Alden takes another pause, and rolls his sleeve up once more.

"The scars serve as a reminder - a reminder of what happens if I am to fail again, but also as a reminder that I have been given a second chance - a chance to prevent something like that from happening ever again. Like you, Calarel, we have both been given a second chance, and neither one of us will be late once more."

When he turned from her, pulling his sleeve back down like something ashamed to be seen, she remained quiet for a long moment. Then she moved just a few steps until she stood beside him, close enough that he would know he wasn’t standing there alone.

“Elena,” she repeated softly, as if committing the name to memory. Her eyes lingered briefly on the sleeve he had lowered.

“My sister’s name is Yvette.” The name came out steadier than the memories behind it. “She was younger than me. Loud, stubborn…entirely convinced she was smarter than everyone else in the room…despite how frail she was.” A faint ghost of a smile flickered at the corner of her mouth. “Most of the time she was right.” Her eyes drifted toward the dark window. “She believed the world was bigger than Cindros. She wanted to see it all.” 

A quiet breath escaped her. “Turns out the world came for her first.” Her jaw tightened slightly. “The Saintillians raided Cindros, and they took her.” She delivered her words plainly, but beneath them something burned— an inferno carefully contained behind the calm mask she wore. “My parents didn’t survive the night.”

Silence stretched between them for a moment. Then Calarel spoke again.

“You walked into a burning building to save someone you loved.” Her eyes drifted to the scars he had tried to hide. “That is devotion. It is that same spark in you that will keep history from repeating itself.” 

A faint edge returned to her voice, but her anger was not meant for him. “I am devoted to finding Yvette. And if Saintillians stand between me and my sister…I will cut my way through every last one of them to bring her home.”


### &#x270D; Alden

“Yvette,” he repeats, the same way she had done for Elena - careful, deliberate. "A beautiful name."

His gaze steadies on Calarel, something firmer behind it now - not just grief, but purpose.

“You’re not wrong to feel that way,” Alden continues. “To fight. To want to tear through whatever stands in your path to get her back.” A small pause. “That kind of devotion… it’s not something to bury.”

“But don’t let it be the only thing that drives you,” he adds, quieter. “Not because it’s wrong - but because it will try to consume everything else if you let it.”

“You’re not alone in this, Calarel,” Alden says. “Not in what you’ve lost… and not in what you’re fighting for.” A faint, steady resolve settles into his voice. “You're not one to give up - not one to lose hope; I know that much. She's still out there somewhere, waiting for you. And if Saintillians stand in the way…”

***Later, after the sewers:***

Calarel stepped out of the sewers at last, wiping a streak of grime from her forearm. Her braid had come loose in places, strands clinging to her face, but she didn’t bother fixing it. For once, her focus wasn’t on the next threat. It was on Alden. She walked a few steps ahead before stopping, then turned back toward him. For a moment, she didn’t speak. She just looked at him.

“You did something back there,” she said finally. “With your magic. I could feel it— the way he reacted when I interrogated him.”

“Thank you.”

She shifted her weight slightly, glancing off toward the distance before looking back again.

“I won’t forget that.”

Something flickered in her expression. Then, with the faintest edge returning:

“Besides,” she added, almost dry, “someone has to make sure your optimism and kind-hearted heroic spirit doesn’t get you killed.”

Alden exhales as he steps fully into the open air, like he’s shedding the weight of the sewers with each breath. What’s left of the sludge clings stubbornly to him, but his posture steadies the moment he’s back under the sky.

At her words, he looks to her - really looks this time - making sure she’s unhurt.

“I’m glad it helped,” he says simply. “You had him. I just… made sure he felt it.”

“…Case in point.”

A small pause, then his tone softens - more personal now.

### &#x270D; Calarel

Calarel watched Alden for a moment, her gaze becoming less and less guarded each time she looked at him. Even with the sewer sludge sticking to him, he looked sort of like an angel to her. At least, the ones she’d pictured in her head as a child before the world had shown her how wicked real angels could be. For a moment, her stomach clenched as she remembered his fall into the well, and she looked him over carefully to make sure he was safe now. Her eyes drifted away just as he looked to her, missing it by mere milliseconds.

“No, he certainly can’t,” she confirmed with a quiet shake of her head, then corrected herself after a beat, “Not from *us*.” She stepped closer as he finished speaking. Although he had told her she didn’t owe him anything, she still reached up. A cloth slid from her shoulders into her hand as she lifted it, carefully brushing grime from his forehead before moving to clean what she could from his armor. 

 “Callie.” The name landed softly between them. Her hand paused for half a second before continuing its work, as though she had only just decided to say it aloud. “That’s what I was called before all of this. It’s not exactly intimidating, so don’t make a habit of using it around anyone else.” Her eyes flicked up to his briefly, measuring him in that quiet way of hers. She stepped back then, reclaiming just a little space between them. 

“I suppose you’re right.” She huffed a laugh. “And don’t worry,” she added, a flicker of dry humor returning as she tilted her head slightly and returned the wink. “Nothing is too difficult for me.”
