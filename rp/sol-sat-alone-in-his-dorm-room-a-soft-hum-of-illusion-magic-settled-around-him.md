### &#x270D; Sol/Sunni

Sol sat alone in his dorm room; a soft hum of illusion magic settled around him like a second heartbeat. Sol’s room felt like stepping into a constellation. Anyone entering for the first time would pause at the threshold. 

The ceiling wasn’t really a ceiling at all. A living night sky stretched overhead, painted with drifting auroras of violet, sapphire, and soft emerald. Stars flickered in shifting constellations, subtly rearranging themselves depending on the mood of whoever occupied the space. Tonight they pulsed slowly and uncertainly, reflecting him more than he realized. 

A warmth radiated through the room, softened by a gentle, magically sustained breeze that carried the faint scent of vanilla and honey. It lazily moved the sheer, celestial-themed fabrics hanging from the base of his lofted, canopy bed. Navy and gold silks pooled across the mattress like liquid twilight. A series of short, prismatic stairs led up to the loft. Each step caught the ambient light, splitting it into tiny rainbows on the walls. 

Beneath the loft, Sol sat on a plush, white couch. A low table stood in front of him, holding a neat stack of books on illusion and enchantment magic, their gilded titles catching the light as the auroras shifted above. Below the table stretched a velvety black rug, thick enough to sink toes into and forget the world outside. Along the wall to his right, hooks displayed Sol’s instruments. A small gap sat between the lyre and the flute, the spot where his guitar usually hung, now resting in his lap. 

On the opposite side of the room, past the couch, stood his creative workspace: a carefully organized crafting corner. A mannequin waited, draped in half-finished fabric, spools of thread lined up by hue and metallic shimmer, and delicate tools rested on his desk in deliberate order. 

Sol’s fingers drifted across the guitar strings, coaxing out a soft melody. His mind replayed the last few days: sparring matches, revelations, fights, celebrations, and all the quiet moments in between where Caspian seemed just out of reach. He couldn’t stop thinking about Caspian’s strange behavior: the schedule changes, the steadfast defense of a mean girl, the uncharacteristic hiding from Diana, the subtle changes in his aura. Sol wasn’t upset, but the distance his friend was keeping from him frightened him more than he wanted to admit. 

He set the guitar aside, its last chord fading gently into the warmer air of the room. Then he closed his eyes and focused on Caspian. He softly guided his thoughts outward, like knocking gently on a door. 

> *“Cas? Hey, if you’re not busy, could we talk? I’m in my room.”*

He had no judgment or impatience tone in his message, just quiet sincerity and a flicker of worry he couldn’t quite hide. 

Sol opened his eyes again, watching the auroras shift overhead as he waited for Caspian’s reply.

His voice softened even further.

“I think you came into my life so I wouldn’t face this alone. And maybe,” he gave Caspian a small smile, “so you wouldn’t have to either.”

“If you’re the compass in all this,” he gestured lightly toward Caspian, half-teasing, half-affection, “then I guess I’ll make sure there’s always something worth navigating toward.”

Then, he hugged Caspian again, and barely above a whisper, but carrying more weight than any spell, he said: “I’m glad to call you my brother.”

The auroras above changed colors to peach, gold, and dusty pink, and the air seemed to warm around them; it was as if the magic of the room itself acknowledged the newly formed family.
