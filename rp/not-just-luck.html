Not Just Luck

April 21, 2026 – April 22, 2026
Calarel

Calarel didn’t slow when she spoke; she just angled a glance over her shoulder, her braid shifting against the line of her back as the last of the tavern’s noise spilled out into the street behind them. The door thudded shut behind them, cutting the sound short.

“You made some bold moves while gambling today, Laenor,” she said, voice low and dry, though there was the faintest curl of approval tucked into it. “Sorry it didn’t work out for you this time. Not that Lady Luck was showing me her favor either.” One shoulder lifted in a faint, dismissive shrug, as if luck had never held much weight with her to begin with.

She finally stopped under the wash of a crooked lantern, pivoting cleanly on her heel before turning to face him fully. The dim gold light caught strangely against her icy blue skin, softening the sharper edges of her features. Her lavender eyes flicked over him, measuring, but not unkind.

“Either way,” she added, “it seems worth finding a good ale for two drinking buddies, wouldn’t you agree?”

She jerked her chin down the street, toward a quieter stretch of town where the noise thinned and the night settled in closer. A corner of her mouth tugged upward, just enough to count as a smile if someone was paying attention.

“Well?” Her gaze lingered on Laenor a second longer, steady and expectant. “Are you coming, or do you only take risks when there’s something to win?”

Even as she asked, she was already half-turned toward the street, as if she expected him to follow and would keep walking whether he did or not.

Laenor let the question linger in the night’s hush before answering. His boots struck the street with an unhurried rhythm as he fell into step behind her, a soft huff of amusement escaping at her words.

“Bold,” he said, “That’s an interesting way of dressing up what most people would call catastrophic timing and monumentally poor judgment.”

His gaze lingered briefly on her posture, noting how she held herself with a calm confidence, seemingly unbothered by the world as if it rarely pressed hard enough to leave a mark.

“Luck’s a fickle thing,” he continued,his voice dropping an octave. “I’ve never trusted it enough to mourn when it turns its back.”

Laenor’s gaze flicked down the street she’d indicated, then back to her, catching that near-smile before it could fully vanish.

He closed the remaining distance in two smooth strides, the shift in proximity entirely intentional, a physical counterpoint to her challenge. He let his gaze drop, just for a moment, to the corner of her mouth where that smile had been, before locking eyes with her again.

“An ale with you?” he said, voice lowering slightly, “Lead the way, Calarel,” he added, glancing sideways at her. “I am deeply curious to see if your standards for ale are better than your luck at the tables.”

Calarel

Calarel didn’t slow when Laenor closed the distance.

“Catastrophic timing and monumentally poor judgment, hmm?” she echoed, dry as old wine. “Your words, not mine. Though…you could just take the compliment.”

Her gaze flicked sideways, catching him in her periphery rather than granting him the full satisfaction of her attention. The ghost of that almost-smile returned, sharper this time, edged with teeth.

She turned down the street she’d chosen without breaking pace, trusting he’d follow. The lamplight caught briefly in her eyes, with a flash of something colder beneath the humor. Perhaps the world had indeed made its mark. She tracked the drop in his voice as he spoke of luck, as if the darkness had found him, too.

At his remark about the ale, that near-smile finally settled in properly, though it didn't quite soften her expression. It simply made it more dangerous, if anything.

A pause as she pushed open the tavern door, turning just enough to brace it with her back, holding it open until he stepped through. Heat and noise rolled out to meet them, thick with smoke and low conversation. The kind of place where no one asked questions unless they were ready for the answer.

“Oh, don’t worry,” she said, tone edged with something almost like amusement. “I have impeccable taste in ale. It’s the company I keep that’s questionable. But…if it doesn’t meet your expectations, I’ll let you pretend it’s my fault. You seem like you’d enjoy that.”

Laenor stepped through the threshold, the hush of the street giving way to a dense wash of heat, smoke, and the scent of roasted grain that clung to the air like a second skin. He didn't miss the way she braced the oak with her back, which felt more like a test than a courtesy.

“Questionable company?” he repeated. His voice barely a murmur, almost lost beneath the tavern’s hum, as his gaze drifted across the room. Shadowed faces, quiet corners, the kind of place where attention was currency, best spent sparingly. Not common, but not fully unfamiliar to him.

He didn’t linger. Spotting a table tucked in a warm corner, just far enough from the center to avoid any curious ears. He moved ahead and claimed it. The chair scraped softly as he pulled it out, a silent offer with a tilt of his hand.

"I'll admit," he said, glancing at the bar before meeting her eyes again. "This isn't quite what I pictured for your haunt, but I'll indulge you… I'm intrigued."

A slow curl tugged at the corner of his mouth.

“So,” he added, voice dipping just slightly, “I’ll leave it to you. What does Calarel order here? Dealer’s choice! It feels appropriate after the evening we’ve had, doesn’t it? ”

He held her gaze a moment longer before easing into his own seat, eagerly awaiting her response to his “challenge”.
