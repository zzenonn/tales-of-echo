*~Six Days After the Quasi Incident~*

Sol had settled down in a courtyard: the stone still held warmth from the afternoon sun, ivy threaded up the walls in lazy spirals, leaves stirred whenever a breeze wandered through, and for once, no voices were drifting from lecture halls or the training grounds. There were no bells dismissing students, just quiet.

He balanced his sketchbook against his knee, and his hands moved without much thought. Usually, days like this would’ve felt so freeing, but now it felt hollow. Peace wasn’t fun when you knew the cause of it.

*Five days,* he thought absently, shading in a curve for an archway. *Five days since the Quasi implosion.*

His mind slipped back there: the flickers of unstable magic, the pressure in his chest, the sound of things breaking, and through all of it, the way Adrien and Zurr’ka had thrown themselves into danger without hesitation.

Sol’s pencil slowed, then stilled. “They didn’t have to,” he murmured under his breath.

He flipped to a fresh page, hoping to reset the feeling in his chest. This time the lines came out softer, more intentional: strings of lanterns sketched between trees, a small table tucked beneath the shade, four chairs angled toward one another. It looked peaceful, almost laughably normal.

Normal sounded nice right about now. Adrien’s smile crept into his mind, followed by the memory of Zurr’ka standing his ground when everything went wrong. He thought about the relief Sol had knowing Redd and Zurr’ka had finally made things right between them. After everything they’d all survived, it felt wrong to sit in the silence and let the days blur past.

*I want to do something for them,* Sol thought. Something thoughtful, a thank you that didn’t involve risking their lives.

He tapped the pencil against the page, thinking through many options. Illusory lanterns, something warm to eat. Maybe they could do something in a courtyard, or maybe in the gardens if they were open. He wanted somewhere open to the sky, where nothing felt trapped or heavy.

Sol exhaled, shoulders loosening as the idea settled. “Yeah,” he said quietly to himself, gaze drifting from the sketchbook to the empty path leading into the courtyard. “That could work.”

He adjusted his grip on the pencil, adding another small detail to the drawing, and waited, half-aware of the space beside him, as if expecting footsteps to break the quiet at any moment.
