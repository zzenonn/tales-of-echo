Redd had commandeered a library table that was technically meant for four people...

The Spark Saga · Redd Auri Vornakir
January 12, 2026 – January 19, 2026
Redd

Redd had commandeered a library table that was technically meant for four people and practically meant for two plus a reckless number of books. Several tomes were stacked in uneven towers around him—fiend classifications, historical incidents in the Obsidian Barricade, one that looked suspiciously like it hadn’t been opened past the table of contents. Notes were scattered between them like casualties.

He glanced up at Auri, then immediately let out a deep sigh.

“Okay. Thanks again for doing this with me,” he said, lowering his voice out of habit rather than necessity. “Research is… not my strongest survival instinct.”
He gestured vaguely at the papers in front of him, one of which had a very judgmental ink blot.

“I did spectacularly bad on the written portion of my midterms. Like—impressively so.” A beat. “Thank every god listening that the practical was half the grade, or Animal and Nature Studies would’ve taken me out back, and that would have been incredibly embarrassing for a Druid”

Redd leaned back in his chair, balancing it briefly on two legs before letting it thump back down.

“Bs get degrees and all.” He winced theatrically. “C in Poli Sci, though. Apparently ‘strong opinions delivered confidently’ is not the same thing as knowing what I’m talking about.”

He tilted his head toward the pile labeled Kalen Fiends, squinting at a paragraph like it might flinch first.

“So. Yes. I am very grateful you’re here to stop me from confidently writing something deeply wrong.” A crooked grin followed. “Please interrupt me at any point if I start inventing facts.”

She planted her elbows on the table, resting her chin in her hands, and shook her head once. “So now,” she continued, glancing at him with a wry expression. “You don’t get to spiral alone. We can be disappointed together.”

Auri pulled one of the tomes at random and opened it. It was, unsurprisingly, also about fiends. “But,” she said lightly, “I’m here. Think of me as your scholarly impulse control.” She skimmed a paragraph, then looked back up at him. “Alright,” she said. “Show me what you’re working on.” He teased her, causing her to playfully roll her eyes and grin. “Please, you know I’ll have no qualms about interrupting you if you start inventing facts.”

Redd

Redd flipped one of the heavier tomes around and squinted at a passage he’d already underlined twice, then cleared his throat and read aloud anyway.

“‘Kalen Fiends originating from the Obsidian Barricade exhibit patterns consistent with external direction, suggesting sustained control by an unidentified intelligence rather than independent volition.’”

He stopped, stared at the page, then let his forehead dip forward until it lightly thunked against the book.

“Great,” he muttered. “So they’re dangerous, summoned, and being puppeted by someone we still can’t name. Riveting. Truly. I love learning the same thing five different ways.”

Redd dragged the book back into its stack with a sigh, fingers raking briefly through his hair as if he could physically shake a new insight loose. “I swear, if one more scholar or archmage says ‘unidentified intelligence’, I’m going to throw up.”

*As he reached for another volume, his hand paused.

His eyes caught on a different spine wedged between two far more ominous-looking texts. The title was unmistakable.*

A Field Guide to Drow Courtship (Revised Edition).

Redd blinked once. Then the corner of his mouth curved, slow and unmistakably pleased.

“Well,” he said, tone immediately lighter, tapping the spine with one finger, “this is suddenly a much more interesting section of the Obsidian Barricade.”

He glanced up at Auri, brows lifting innocently. “Technically,” he added, “it’s still regional research.” A beat. “And given your… current extracurricular associations with a certain drow from that region, I’d argue this counts as being academically responsible.”

Redd slid the book partway out, glancing down at it like an old acquaintance. “Revised edition, too,” he noted. “Always good when a culture updates its expectations.”

He tipped his head, smirk deepening. “Tell you what—before I lose the will to live to another paragraph about fiends being ‘mysterious,’ we take a quick break. Purely educational.” A pause, then, casually: “I want to see your reaction when you read some of this shit.”

That earned him a sideways look of his own making, and he grinned wider.

“Trust me,” he said lightly. “This one’s going to be relevant.”

Redd

Redd watched the book disappear into the stack, lips pressed together like he was honoring the decision.

Then he exhaled through his nose and tipped his head back slightly.

“Come oooon,” he said, making it clear he had absolutely no intention of letting this go.

He leaned forward again, resting his forearms on the table, eyes flicking pointedly toward the pile where the book now hid. “I hear everything you’re saying,” he continued, tone earnest in a way that was very obviously insincere. “This is not about Vornakir. This is about the Obsidian Barricade. Regional context. Cultural literacy. Very important. Completely unrelated to the fact that your… hypothetical interest happens to be a drow from there.”

A pause. A grin.

“And anyway,” Redd added, “you’re the one who said reading counts as 'acceptable leisure'. This is reading.” He held up a finger. “Five minutes. No towns or schools in danger. We’ll go right back to fiends and their mysterious, ever-unidentified puppet masters after. I promise.”

He reached over and nudged the book back into the open space between them, deliberately stopping short of opening it himself. “Besides,” he said wiggling his eyebrows, “don't you want to know what they do with the masks and cords??"

At her look—Redd’s grin widened. “Nope. Not explaining.” He leaned back in his chair, folding his arms with exaggerated patience. “I could tell you. Or you could read it.”

He lifted his brows, clearly enjoying himself. “Five minutes,” he repeated.

“Alright,” he said, eyes dropping briefly to his notes as if consulting them for a diagnosis. “If you’re insane, it’s doing a very bad job of showing.” He glanced back up at her. “You read all that and immediately started weighing intent, consent, consequences, and long-term compatibility. Token given, worn publicly, intent acknowledged—even if neither of you framed it as a ritual at the time.”

He shrugged, thoughtful rather than certain. “You didn’t set out to perform it, but the structure’s there.” He tapped the quill once against the table. “Sometimes the ritual works even when the people involved aren’t trying to invoke it. Although you may wanna have a conversation with Vornakir about it.”

He flipped a page, scanning a paragraph, then spoke again, tone still easy. “For what it’s worth,” he said, “Vornakir and I did talk about you.” He glanced up quickly, reading her expression. “I'm not gonna go into specifics that were said in confidence.”

Redd underlined a sentence, the scratch of the quill soft in the quiet. “But was very clear about one thing,” he continued. “That it would be something he may choose to go through with you. And that he would never put you in danger.”

He met her eyes again. “So talk to him. He's not the type to make you do things you'll be uncomforable with” His voice softened. “Those rituals are meant to force honesty before commitment.”

Then, deliberately lighter, he reached for the next book. “Alright,” he said, opening it. “Before we accidentally solve drow courtship instead of Kalen Fiends—” a glance, amused “—let’s get back to mysterious external intelligences and academic despair.”
