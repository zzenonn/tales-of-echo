The Celstate Saga · Talys Calarel
March 11, 2026 – March 24, 2026
Talys

The ruins of the bridge grew distant in the background. Blue Squadron escorted the majority of the troops stationed there back to Cindros proper. Their formation was spread out and since there had been no aerial pursuit there the only urgency remaining was that of the soldiers below marching to their nights rest.

Talys and Biter glided softly above the trees and road. Carving long banking turns that creating a spiraling path forward. They checked each flank and stayed in a flexible position should an ambush arise. However once the walls came into view they were within a safe range for reinforcements and he was finally able to relax. Directing course for Tower Grandveil he found himself next to Calarel and Baelor.

The rain had thankfully begun to wash away sweat, blood, and sewer grime from the two riders but the remnants still remained on both of them. Talys urged Biter closer, knowing the wyvern wasn’t dumb enough to snap at Baelor. He waved down Calarel’s attention.

“Quite the fireworks display you got to set off there. I’m a little jealous, you seemed to be enjoying yourself when it finally went off. I didn’t peg you for someone to enjoy such things. Or perhaps it’s our particular foe that has you so thoroughly satisfied?”

Never missing a beat the probing question that ended his statement appeared like a viper. Talys may not be the most persuasive or the most intimidating, but he was a talented conversationalist and each word was measured, weighed, and executed with precision.

“That and you were very… specific in your interrogation in the sewers. A personal matter?”

The smile never left his face and he didn’t see upset at all. Behind his eyes was curiosity and excitement. He waited with a small degree of anticipation for her response.

Calarel

Rain slid in thin, silver lines across the sky as Vaelor’s wings beat steadily beside Biter’s. The wyvern cut through the mist with immense strength, the air rushing past them on cool currents. The smell of smoke and damp stone still clung faintly.

She noticed Talys guiding Biter closer before he even spoke. Vaelor’s head turned slightly in acknowledgment of the other wyvern but showed no sign of agitation. The two beasts flew parallel, their wings rising and falling in a slow, powerful rhythm.

Calarel shifted slightly in the saddle as Talys waved her attention over. Her dark hair, heavy with rain, clung in damp strands along her neck and jaw. When he began speaking, she listened without interruption, her expression calm despite the teasing tone. At the comment about the explosion, one brow lifted faintly.

“Enjoying myself?” She repeated, the words almost lost beneath the rush of wind. The corner of her mouth raised just slightly. “I did, yes. It was quite…effective. Wouldn’t you agree?”

Talys’ next question caused her fingers to tighten subtly on Vaelor’s reins. For a moment she didn’t answer. Rain continued to fall between them, drumming against scales and leather. Vaelor’s gave a giant exhale, a plume of warm breath vanishing into the mist.

“Personal,” she confirmed. Her gaze held his for a moment longer than necessary, measuring him in the same way he had measured her questions.

“But yes,” she continued, the edge of something quieter entering her tone. “I’m looking for my sister. So if I seemed…insistent with the Saintillian,” she said calmly, “now you know why.”

“ I’m surprised you noticed. I assumed you were too busy making yourself at home in the sewer, seeing as you’re a self-proclaimed expert on the topic.”

A laugh of amusement followed.
“Though I suppose,” she added, glancing at him again with a wink, “everyone should have at least one area of expertise.”

Mar 13, 2026 · 10:29 PM UTC
Talys

Talys did not react much to Calarel sharing that her sister was in the hands of the Saintillians. He did let his smile fade a tad, not wanting to seem to take pleasure in the information.

When her spirits returned to her so did Talys’s smirk. He liked Calarel, she had never balked at his teasing the way most did. She kept her cool and even played along like now. For someone so serious most of the time she was quite fun the rest of the time.

“Effective is putting it lightly Calarel. It was an explosive success as they say. I had a blast.”

Talys chuckled at his own jokes.

“So you don’t have to undersell it even if it’s a little grim. If you enjoyed that bridge exploding then own it. We ride giant apex predators around after locking ourselves in a room with them for months. We all have at least some kind of screw loose.”

He patted Biter on the neck. The wyvern gave his jaws a snap of endearment towards Talys even though he couldn’t reach anywhere near him in flight.

“A sister in the hands of the enemy though. Quite a tragedy. An insistence you have every right to now that I know the circumstances.”

He left it at that though he filed away the fact knowing that the Saintillians don’t take prisoners without good reason. And that this had seemed to have happened long before the war began.

“The sewers on the other hand are just the beginning of my expertise. They aren’t home per se but I guess you could call me nostalgic. As a boy and young man I did a fair bit of work that had me traveling the sewers. My mother hated the stench but bringing home some silver pieces smoothed it over most nights.”

Nostalgia had retaken him for a moment. A simpler time in his life was appreciated given the hard decisions made today.

Mar 18, 2026 · 06:27 PM UTC
Calarel

Calarel listened to Talys without interruption, the steady rhythm of Vaelor’s wings carrying them forward through the rain as he spoke. The faintest shift in his expression at her admission didn’t go unnoticed, though she didn't comment on it. At his string of notably terrible puns, however, her brow arched.

“...An explosive success,” she repeated flatly, the faint curve at the corner of her mouth betraying her. “You’ve been waiting all night to say that, haven’t you?”

His next words drew a quieter reaction. She didn’t immediately look at him, her gaze instead fixed ahead where the lights of the city bled gold through the rain. Gold. Her least favorite color.

“Enjoyment, yes,” she said after a moment. “Satisfaction, too. It needed to be done.”

Her tone remained even. There was no apology in it, just the truth, cleanly stated.

Then the conversation shifted to her sister, and her grip on the reins steadied.

“Tragedy implies finality,” she replied. “I haven’t accepted that. I won’t accept that.”

A quiet certainly lived in the words, not defiance, not desperation. Something…colder and enduring.

Then Talys shifted, and with it, the tone. Calarel glanced over as he spoke of the sewers with something thoughtful and assessing. The pieces were fitting together. The smirk, the ease, the way he moved through the world like nothing could truly unsettle him…it rearranged itself slightly in her mind.

“...Nostalgic,” she echoed softly. “I wouldn’t have guessed.”

A beat passed, wind rushing between them, rain beading along her hair and skin.

“But it explains a few things. Your comfort in confined spaces,” she continued. “Your talent for finding paths others overlook.”

Her eyes flicked toward him, holding his for just a second.

“And your remarkable ability to make yourself at home wherever you land.”

The words could have been teasing, but instead they were simply quiet acknowledgement. As the towers rose closer, cutting through the mist, she looked to him.

“If you’re going to insist on keeping me company,” she said calmly, “You’ll have to do better than terrible puns.”

“And for what it’s worth,” she added after a moment. “I’d consider those pieces well earned, even if your mother disagreed with your methods. All we can do is the best we can.”

A slight tilt of her head followed her words, the ghost of challenge in it.

“I expect your best, Tayls.”
