Redd’s boots carried him down the hall in sharp, steady rhythm, the kind of rhythm he used when he needed people to believe he was fine. He’d worn the mask all the way from the arena — face set, smile clipped, even trading nods with classmates still buzzing about the exhibition. He’d returned a half-grin when someone congratulated him on his own match — the best fight of his life, clean and decisive. For a fleeting moment, he’d thought the weeks of pushing himself had meant something.

But when the dorm door clicked shut behind him, the high shattered.

The common room waited in quiet disarray; books stacked, mugs abandoned, the lamp’s glow stretching shadows too far across the walls; remnants of the mess in the kitchen he made when he and Zurr’ka tried to make Ratatouille; Caspian's weapons that Redd tried to use to cut the vegetables

. All those happy moments seem like a distant memory now.

Redd stood just inside, hands twitching at his sides before dragging them through his hair. His reflection in the darkened window showed the hollows beneath his eyes, the cost of nights spent hammering himself into a better duelist. He’d trained until the small hours, over and over again, dueling until his arms shook and his throat was raw, all to raise his rank, to be stronger than the version of himself who had once failed.

And today, he had done it. His best match yet. A high he hadn’t felt in months.

Then Sorjin. Gods, Sorjin.

The memory crashed over him: stepping out as a surprise opponent. Sorjin, who wasn’t a spellcaster, who never should’ve had that kind of power. And then the fireball. One blazing eruption, fast and merciless, a one-hit blow that sent Griffin crumpling in smoke and ash. Not dead thanks to Sunny, but the sight of him sprawled, chest heaving, still seared behind Redd’s eyes.

Redd stumbled to the couch, clutching its back like an anchor. His words broke out jagged, half-formed. “No, that’s not, he can’t . . . he’s never been . . .” His voice faltered, thoughts slipping faster than he could catch them.

The cycle. He felt it tearing through him. After Velvyn, he’d nearly broken — but at least he could blame her, curse her for what she’d done. After Vornakir, he’d carried the guilt of not being strong enough to stop it, but at least there’d been the fucking Beholder Zombie to hate, a monster to pin it on.

But now? There was no one else. No betrayer. No beast. Just Sorjin. and the blame that led straight back to Redd.

His breath came fast, ragged, his shoulders shaking as he pressed his palms to the table. “Not again,” he whispered, voice raw and cracking. “Gods, not again.”

He had no answers. Sorjin shouldn’t have been able to do that. He shouldn’t have had that kind of power. The things Sorjin must have done to gain that kind of power so quickly . . . Redd’s stomach twisted. He’d read of infernal creatures before, things that bite deep, that feed on the desperate and the vulnerable

. To think one of them might have slithered into the school, making bargains in the shadows?

And if Sorjin had become its pawn, what stopped it from reaching further? What stopped it from setting its eyes on Caspian, on Zašia, on Vornakir—on Zurr’ka?

The name slipped free, unguarded, a whisper that hurt more than the heat of the fireball itself. “Zurr’ka . . .”

Exhaustion pulled at every muscle, dragging him lower, but his fear kept him pacing, restless, hollow. He had nothing left — no plan, no direction, no way forward. Just the same brutal pattern: the climb, the crash, the wreckage left in his wake.

And this time, Redd Ravenshire had no one to blame but himself.


### &#x270D; Sol/Sunni
<sub>Sep 21, 2025 &middot; 11:46 PM UTC</sub>

The sound of steps in the common room drew Sol from the hall. He paused in the doorway, taking in the sight of Redd moving across the space, then quietly made his way to the couch. Settling onto its edge, he leaned forward, hands clasped loosely between his knees, and watched for a moment.

“You’ll wear a hole in the floor at this rate,” Sol said gently, letting a thin smile curve at the corner of his mouth. His voice softened as the words left him. “Want to tell me what’s wrong?”

Behind the calm tone, his thoughts turned. He knew that pace, that jagged rhythm of someone carrying too much weight. He’d worn it himself often enough. Sol wasn’t sure what storm Redd was caught in, but he knew the shape of the silence around it. So he left the question open; an invitation without pressure, a space where maybe Redd could lay some of it down.


### &#x270D; Redd
<sub>Sep 22, 2025 &middot; 03:18 AM UTC</sub>

Redd froze mid-step at Sol’s voice. His head dipped, hair falling into his face, and for a moment he almost didn’t answer. Then he gave a thin, humorless laugh.

“You really picked the worst night to walk in, Sunny.” His voice was raw, uneven. “You don’t know me. Don’t know any of this. Maybe that’s why I should keep my mouth shut. Because if I tell you... you’ll probably hate me for it. And gods, I don’t want that. Not when you’ve only just stepped into this madhouse of a school and,” He broke off, shoulders rising and falling too fast.

He hesitated, fingers flexing against the table like he could hold the words back. “Still... you’re here now. You’ve sat at this table, stepped into our chaos—Aurum, murders, school, all of it. That makes you one of us. And if that’s true, then you deserve the truth.”

He braced both hands on the table, knuckles tight. “It started with Velvyn. She wanted dirt on Sorjin. I wanted information on Maelys Dray, one of the cursed skulls. So I made a deal. I’d get her Sorjin’s thesis, she’d give me what I wanted.”

Redd’s jaw worked as he started pacing again. “But Sorjin wasn’t going to just hand that over. So I... I went after Zurr’ka. He was the one Sorjin wanted. And I played it up. Every smile, every touch, every chance I had to make him watch, I took it. Until Sorjin gave me what I asked for, just to be rid of me.”

His throat bobbed. “And the thing is, his thesis wasn’t even dangerous. He wanted to take the fungus from the Obsidian Barricade, turn it into a way to share messages with the world. But Velvyn; she took it as an insult, an attack on her people. She didn’t care about the work, only that it was a slight. And I... I gave her more. Sorjin’s whereabouts. In exchange for her spreading rumors about my dueling, so I’d look good before sponsorship day.”

Redd’s hands shook. “She found him and beat him badly. And it all happened because of me. All my fault”

His pacing slowed, voice rough. “After Velvyn, I told myself I could blame her. After Vornakir lost his arm, I told myself I could blame the beholder zombie. But now?” He shook his head. “There’s no one else. Just Sorjin. And me.”

The silence stretched, heavy. His voice fell low. “And then, of course, I went and made it worse. I ended up actually liking Zurr’ka. More than that. Enough that he matters now, more than anything. But the damage was already done. Sorjin’s broken, twisted into something else, and the thread runs back to me.”

His pacing slowed until he just stood there, staring down at his trembling hands. The silence pressed in, heavy and unkind. For years, he’d told himself he was the victim; cast by his father from Luminar, toyed with by Velvyn, left helpless when Vornakir nearly died. It was easier that way. Easier to believe he was the one being pushed around by forces larger than him.

Redd finally turned to Sol, this newcomer who had wandered into his wreckage without knowing any of it. His voice dropped low, almost fragile. “You healed Griffin tonight. You saved him. That was good. You’re good. Me... I don’t even know anymore.” His throat tightened as he forced the last words out. “From the outside looking in... do you think I’m a good person?”


### &#x270D; Sol/Sunni
<sub>Sep 22, 2025 &middot; 04:18 AM UTC</sub>

Sol rose from the couch slowly, wings shifting in a soft rustle as he crossed the space between them. He didn’t reach out immediately; he just stopped close enough that Redd would feel his presence like a steady anchor. His voice, when it came, was calm but firm.

“Even good people can make mistakes, Redd. And yeah, sometimes those mistakes hurt others. But it’s not about what you did anymore, it’s about what you’re going to do from here. That’s what matters.”

He tilted his head, searching Redd’s face with steady, golden eyes. “Even when you mask it under all that bravado, all I’ve seen from you since I got here is someone who makes people laugh, someone who cares enough to keep showing up. That’s not nothing. That’s not bad.” His mouth tugged into a small, sincere smile. “Sure, you did a messed-up thing. But you *learned* from it, didn’t you? That’s more than most can say.”

Sol let one hand rest lightly on the table between them, grounding the words. “And remember, people are free to make their own choices. Sorjin’s outburst? That wasn’t you pulling the strings. That was his choice, and he’ll have to live with the backlash. You don’t have to carry all of that weight.”

Finally, he closed the last of the distance, his hand resting lightly on Redd’s shoulder, meant to remind him he wasn’t alone. “Redd, even good people screw up. Sometimes badly. And yeah, it hurts others. But that’s the beauty of being alive. We’re all going to break things, we’re all going to stumble, but what separates good from bad is what you do with the pieces afterward.”

He let that hang in the air, his eyes searching Redd’s face with quiet conviction. “All I’ve seen from you is someone who fights to keep going, someone who cares more than he wants to admit. From where I stand, you’re not some monster. You’re Redd, and you’re my friend.”

And somehow… I haven’t drowned yet.”

He ran his fingers absently along the arm of the couch, thoughtful. “Friends came quicker than I expected. You’re jokester self. Caspian is loud as thunder, and drinks like he’s storing wine for winter. Zašia is… sharp. Elegant. Loyal to a fault. And Vornakir is regal, exhausting at times, but grounded. He doesn’t bend, and that’s something.” His voice softened. “Griffin’s good, through and through. He makes you want to believe the world isn’t all teeth and claws.”

When Redd stumbled over the name, Sol laughed loudly and genuinely. “Adrien. His name’s Adrien,” he corrected, voice lighter than before. His gaze dipped, tracing the shimmer of the lake in his mind. “He’s in illusions class with me. He’s kinda clumsy with it, his threads fray every time he tries to hold an image, but gods, he tries. I got close, helped him settle his hand, his focus. You know how it feels when you see someone fighting to find their spark? It’s… endearing.”

Sol shook his head, almost amused at himself. “He’s got this quiet way about him. Like, he doesn’t think he deserves to take up space, but when he does, you want him to stay there. He smiles, and it feels like you’ve been let in on something private. I don’t know if that makes sense.” He gave a helpless shrug, cheeks warming a shade. “But I like being around him. More than I should admit.”

He glanced back at Redd then, eyes catching his with an earnest steadiness. “So that’s me, apparently. The boy with too many friends, too many feelings, and wings too big for most doorways. Spark-Oath hasn’t scared me off yet. Maybe because people like you keep making it worth staying.”

Sol’s grin widened as he leaned forward, chin propped on his hand. “You know, I *can* help with chopping,” he said, voice carrying just enough mischief to be a warning.

He released a sharp whistle, and with a glimmer of gold, one of the tomatoes on the counter lifted into the air. It twirled lazily once, then diced itself in a series of neat, precise cuts, each piece dropping into the waiting bowl as if the fruit had volunteered for the task.

Sol arched a brow at Redd, golden eyes catching the lamplight. “Consider it my contribution to your signature dish.”
