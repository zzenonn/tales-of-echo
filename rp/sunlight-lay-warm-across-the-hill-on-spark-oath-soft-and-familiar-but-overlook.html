Sunlight lay warm across the hill on Spark Oath, soft and familiar, but overlook...

The Spark Saga · Redd Caspian
December 16, 2025 – January 10, 2026
Redd

Sunlight lay warm across the hill on Spark Oath, soft and familiar, but overlooking stone and wood different from the usual academy steps. A marble clock tower rang its bell; the Ravenshire manor a silhouette in the distance. Redd lay on the hilltop on top of a picnic blanket.

Fluffy tore past him in a flash of golden fur, paws drumming happily against the stones. Redd laughed—actually laughed—as the dog skidded to a stop and doubled back, tail wagging hard enough to shake his whole body. Nearby, Zurr’ka sat easily on the blanket beside Redd, sunlight catching in his scales. He looked up, smiled, and for a heartbeat the world felt simple.

At the foot of the hill, half out of place and yet unmistakable, stood figures Redd knew by shape alone: Vornakir’s easy posture, Caspian’s steadiness, Suni and Auri close together in quiet conversation.

Redd lifted a hand and waved.

They waved back.

Smiling. Untroubled. Whole.

Something in his chest loosened.

Then the warmth thinned.

Not vanished—just stretched too tight, like glass about to crack.

Molten Redd stood beside him. Suddenly.

“Can't even get your geography right I see,” as looks disapprovingly at the Spark Oath Cliff that overlooked <something no name yet> Village.

Redd turned, frowning. “What—”

“You like moments like this,” Molten Redd said coolly. “They let you pretend you’ve already changed.”

The air shifted.

Wood darkened at the edges. Stone fissured with soft, whispering cracks. Grass dulled from green to gray. No screams—just the slow, terrible certainty of failure unfolding.

Fluffy slowed mid-run. His steps faltered. Golden fur dimmed to ash that scattered on the breeze.

“Fluff—” Redd reached out, too late.

Zurr’ka stood, confusion flickering across his face as the warmth bled out of him. He stepped toward Redd, hand extended—then stopped as fire traced up his arm, not violent, just final. His form thinned, hollowed, and collapsed into pale remnants that scattered across the stones.

Redd’s breath hitched. He turned desperately toward the edge of the square.

Vornakir. Caspian. Suni. Auri.

They were still smiling.

Until the fire reached them.

Their shapes burned down to silhouettes, then to nothing at all, leaving only scorched stone and drifting ash where they had stood moments before.

Redd staggered, heart pounding. “No—no, I can fix this—”

He reached for his fire.

Nothing answered.

No spark. No heat. Just cold ash slipping uselessly through his fingers.

The village continued to burn without him.

Molten Redd watched, expression almost bored.

“Even now,” he said, stepping closer, “you’re too weak to stop it.”

Redd turned in place, panic clawing up his spine. The streets, the buildings, the square—every line of it was unmistakably his. His home. His failure.

Everyone was gone.

Only Redd remained standing amid the ruin.

"This part never lasts. You know that." Molten Redd leaned close, molten eyes bright with certainty, "How long before you ruin this too?"

The ash surged upward, smoke filling Redd’s lungs—

A magma beast roared.

The sound tore through him, deafening and impossibly close, shaking the world apart—

Redd jerked awake with a sharp gasp, heart hammering, sweat cooling against his skin. The darkness of the dorm room pressed in around him, silent except for his ragged breathing and the fading echo of that roar still ringing in his ears.

He lay there for a long moment, staring at the ceiling, chest rising and falling until the panic dulled to something colder and heavier.

Sleep wasn’t coming back.

Redd swung his legs over the side of the bed, pulled on something loose without really looking, and quietly stepped out into the hall. Barefoot, he made his way to the common room, the weight of ash and fire still clinging to him as he crossed the threshold.

Caspian went over to the kitchen and grabbed two mugs and the teapot. If they were both up, might as well have something nice to drink. And there was something off about Redd’s demeanor. He set to work boiling water to make some tea, a habit he’d picked up from his dorm mates.

As the water heated up he turned around and asked a follow up question to his friend.

“Something bothering you? Don’t mean to pry but you seem a little troubled.”

Redd

Redd flinched at the whack, shoulders tensing as Caspian smacked into the doorframe. Instinctively, he took a half-step forward before stopping himself, hands curling uselessly at his sides.

“Sorry,” he said quietly. His voice was rough, like he hadn’t used it in a while. “Didn’t mean to scare you.”

He lingered near the edge of the common room, not quite committing to sitting, gaze following Caspian as he moved into the kitchen. The low light caught the faint sheen of sweat still clinging to Redd’s temples, the way his hair stuck slightly out of place—as if he’d dressed in a hurry and forgotten to care afterward.

At the question, Redd hesitated.

The kettle began to heat. The sound filled the space where an answer should’ve gone.

“Sleep didn’t… stick,” he said finally, a corner of his mouth twitching like it almost remembered how to smile. “Figured I’d stop pretending it would.”

He rubbed at the back of his neck, eyes drifting to the floor, then to the window, anywhere but Caspian’s face. There was a heaviness to him now—less sharp than usual, less rehearsed.

“Just one of those nights,” he added, softer. “Head’s louder than it should be.”

Redd exhaled slowly and leaned against the counter, closer now, close enough to accept the offered normalcy of mugs and tea.

He glanced at Caspian at last, not quite meeting his eyes.

“I don't expect you to do anything about it,” he said. “But… I wouldn’t say no to the company.”
